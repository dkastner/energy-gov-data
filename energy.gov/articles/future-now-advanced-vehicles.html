3 of 10

The Hyundai Tucson

The Hyundai Tucson FCEV is currently available for lease in Southern California for less than $500 per month, including free hydrogen fuel. Hydrogen for FCEVs can be produced from a variety of resources all providing emission reductions. Hydrogen derived from natural gas reduces emissions by half and hydrogen produced from renewables cuts total emissions by more than 90 percent compared to today’s gasoline vehicle. Comparable to today’s gas prices, hydrogen can be produced from low-cost natural gas for approximately $4 per gallon gasoline equivalent when produced at high volumes. Photo by Sarah Gerrity, Energy Department.

Date taken: 2015-01-22 05:11

4 of 10

The Honda Fuel Cell Vehicle

Honda’s fuel cell concept car was unveiled in November 2014 and they predict sales of more than 1,000 vehicles per year for the next five years. Just like traditional internal combustion engines, most fuel cell electric vehicles have a driving range of up to 300 miles on one tank and refuel in less than 5 minutes. Photo by Sarah Gerrity, Energy Department.

Date taken: 2015-01-22 05:08

5 of 10

The Chevrolet Spark

The Chevrolet Spark all-electric vehicle is well-suited for city-dwellers who want to give up gasoline, save money, reduce carbon pollution, and make parking easy. With an annual fuel cost of only $500, drivers will save $4,500 in the first five years of driving according to FuelEconomy.gov. This microcar is the first all-electric vehicle that General Motors produced since the groundbreaking EV1 in the 1990s and uses a GM-produced electric motor built in White Marsh, Maryland. Supported by a DOE Recovery Act project, this is the first factory run by a domestic automaker to build electric motors for a plug-in electric vehicle in the U.S. Photo by Sarah Gerrity, Energy Department.

Date taken: 2015-01-22 04:48

6 of 10

The Chevrolet Volt

While the original Chevrolet Volt was the first plug-in hybrid electric vehicle introduced to the mainstream market, the next-generation one will go on sale in 2016. The new version will now have a 50 mile all-electric range, according to Chevrolet, 12 miles more than the first one. Of course, it will continue to have a back-up gasoline engine if drivers run down that substantial all-electric range. DOE¹s research helped develop both the lithium-ion battery used in the original vehicle as well as the improved version in the next-generation Volt. In fact, research supported by DOE has reduced modeled, high-volume battery costs for plug-in electric vehicles by 40 percent in only three years.
