Ohio State University Races to the Finish as the Winner of EcoCAR 2

June 13, 2014 - 11:01am

Addthis

Year 3 Finish Line Event

The team from the Ohio State University took home the top honors in the Energy Department's EcoCAR 2 competition for its plug-in hybrid electric vehicle that runs on electricity and E85. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

2 of 20

EcoCAR 2 Year 1

EcoCAR 2 is a three-year competition that challenges 15 universities from across North America to reduce the environmental impact of a 2013 Chevrolet Malibu by minimizing the vehicle's fuel consumption and reducing its emissions while retaining the vehicle's performance, safety, and consumer appeal. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

3 of 20

EcoCAR 2 Year 1

To kick off Year 1 of EcoCAR 2, students attended a four-day workshop packed full of sessions designed to provide students with the skills and knowledge they’ll need over the course of the competition. For example, teams learned how to use math-based design tools to compare and select an advanced vehicle powertrain. They also toured GM’s Powertrain Performance Build Center, where they spent time with highly skilled builders while they had the opportunity to assemble sections of an engine. Pictured here are members of the University of Washington team inspecting a Corvette engine in the Powertrain Performance Build Center. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

4 of 20

EcoCAR 2 Year 1

Year 1 of the EcoCAR 2 competition emphasized engineering design. Using modeling and simulation tools, teams selected and virtually tested their plug-in hybrid electric vehicle architecture and started developing some of the major vehicle subsystems. As part of the Year 1 finals in Los Angeles, teams presented their vehicle designs and business plans to more than 100 judges over the course of six days. Pictured here are members of the University of Waterloo team at the Year 1 competition. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

5 of 20

EcoCAR 2 Year 2

The 15 teams competing in EcoCAR 2 converged in Yuma, Arizona, for the Year 2 finals. The two-part competition consisted of on-road vehicle testing at the GM Desert Proving Grounds in Yuma and presentations in San Diego, California. In preparation for dynamic vehicle testing, which began with the crucial first step of a safety inspection, the University of Tennessee team is working on its car in the garage. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

EcoCAR 2 Year 2

Over the span of 10 days, each team’s vehicle underwent engineering tests similar to those conducted by the automotive industry to determine a prototype's readiness for production and ultimately prove the viability of their advanced technology vehicles. Pictured here, members from the Embry Riddle Aeronautical University team work on their car. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

Rose-Hulman Institute of Technology’s vehicle combines an internal combustion engine that runs on E85 (gasoline blended with 85 percent ethanol) and an electric motor to allow the car to operate in two different modes: all-electric and as a hybrid-electric vehicle running on E85. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

EcoCAR 2 Year 3

During Year 3 of EcoCAR 2, students continue to improve their vehicles, refining them into near-production prototypes. The two-part final took place in Milford, Michigan, and Washington, D.C., over 10 days. Pictured here, students from the Ohio State University work on their car in the garage at GM’s Milford Proving Grounds. The Ohio State University team was the first to pass the 300-point safety tech inspection, a requirement before the team could begin an on-road safety evaluation. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

EcoCAR 2 Year 3

EcoCAR 2 challenges university students to also think about a vehicle’s performance, safety and consumer acceptance, and cargo space is a crucial aspect to consumer acceptability. To test their vehicle’s capacity to haul cargo, University of Victoria team members load boxes that mimic a large load in both size and weight. One of the judges is measuring the distance from the bottom of the vehicle to the ground to ensure there is enough clearance when the vehicle is weighed down. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

EcoCAR 2 Year 3

Students from Purdue University work on their vehicle, making last-minute adjustments before a day of tests. Team members reattach the front bumper of their car after making adjustments. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

EcoCAR 2 Year 3

GM professional drivers pushed the teams’ vehicles to the limits to measure the cars’ performance in the Autocross Event (a key part of vehicle testing). The drivers made three attempts through the serpentine coned-course, trying to beat the stock vehicle’s time. For the first time in an Energy Department advanced vehicle competition, all 15 teams qualified to compete in the Autocross Event after having passed both the safety tech and on-road safety evaluation. In the end, Wayne State University’s car had the fastest Autocross time, beating the stock vehicle’s time by nearly 0.6 seconds. | Photo courtesy of EcoCAR 2.

What is EcoCAR 2?

  • A collaboration between the Energy Department and General Motors, EcoCAR 2 challenges collegiate teams to develop innovative ways to improve the fuel economy and reduce the environmental impact of a vehicle without compromising its performance, safety or consumer acceptance.
  • Over the course of three years, teams from 15 colleges across North America have been putting the pedal to the metal designing, building and refining an energy-efficient, consumer-ready alternative fuel vehicle.
  • Ohio State University placed first in the competition with the University of Washington taking second place. Penn State University rounded out the top three.

For the last three years, teams from 15 colleges across North America have been putting the pedal to the metal designing, building and refining an energy-efficient, consumer-ready alternative fuel vehicle as part of the Energy Department’s EcoCAR 2 competition. Yesterday as the competition drew to a close, The Ohio State University was crowned the winner of EcoCAR 2: Plugging into the Future.

EcoCar 2, a collaboration between the Energy Department and General Motors, challenges collegiate teams to develop innovative ways to improve the fuel economy and reduce the environmental impact of a vehicle without compromising its performance, safety or consumer acceptance. Over the course of the competition, EcoCAR 2 put students’ skills to the test while providing them with hands-on, real-world experience to become clean energy leaders.

To start, teams were challenged to incorporate advanced technology components -- including electric drive powertrains (the part of the car that converts power from the fuel source into the mechanical power needed to drive the wheels) and battery systems -- into an existing vehicle. After using software to model their proposed vehicle architecture, GM provided each team with a 2013 Chevrolet Malibu to transform into an advanced plug-in hybrid vehicle that can compete with the most fuel-efficient cars on the market. The teams then followed a real-world engineering process based on the same process GM uses for developing its own vehicles to re-engineer the vehicle with their advanced design.

During EcoCAR 2 Year 1, teams focused on designing their vehicles -- including virtually testing their cars’ architectures and developing all the systems necessary to turn their vehicles into plug-in hybrids. In the second year, teams redesigned their physical vehicles using cutting-edge automotive engineering processes. Year 2 finals culminated with on-road vehicle testing at the same proving grounds course that GM uses to test its own vehicles.

As part of the last year of the competition, students have continued to improve their vehicles, refining them into near-production prototypes. They then traveled to Michigan and Washington, D.C., for a 12-day, two-part finale that included a 300-point safety inspection, a 100-mile emissions and energy consumption test to measure the vehicle’s fuel economy and an autocross event where professional drivers pushed the teams’ cars to the limits, zooming through a serpentine road course.

Throughout the entire competition, students were required to keep their vehicle’s viability and consumer appeal in mind, and judges graded the vehicles on their noise, vibration and overall finish.

Ohio State University’s vehicle came out on top after taking the lead in multiple categories, including best pre-competition safety & technical inspection, lowest criteria emissions and best communications plan. Last night, the team won 14 awards -- and a total of 21 awards over the course of the Year 3 competition -- for their plug-in hybrid electric vehicle that runs on electricity and E85. This setup gives the car about 40 miles of all-electric driving with an additional 150-250 miles of driving using the internal combustion engine.
