Secretary Moniz Opening Remarks at the Wilson Center: “Iran’s Nuclear Agreement”-- As Delivered

February 2, 2016 - 12:00pm

Addthis

Dr. Ernest Moniz
Former Secretary of Energy

SECRETARY ERNEST MONIZ:  Today what we’re going to focus on is basically nuclear materials.  And I would just put that in the context that President Obama, in April of 2009 in the Prague agenda laid out a broad nuclear agenda, with a very, very strong focus on kind of reviving really our programs or actions in terms of controlling, eliminating whenever possible, nuclear materials of risk.  And I’m going to end up today by conjuring up the nuclear security summit that we have coming up at the end of next month, leading in turn, by the way, to the IAEA’s second triannual nuclear security ministerial.  So going from April 2009 to December 2016, I want to emphasize, this is really going to be eight years of really having the nuclear material issue a very, very strong focus of this administration.

Now, of course, there was the, I would say, presumably unanticipated in 2009, the Iran focus – which was fundamentally a discussion about nuclear materials and about the technologies that could make those materials weapons usable, whether it’s high-enriched uranium or plutonium.  But when it comes around to these issues, it roughly speaking always comes back to the availability of nuclear materials and the risks that they can – that they can pose. 

I would note that, because it’s a particular favorite of mine, in this focus of the administration one of the really major events was not the initiation but the conclusion of the HEU with Russia.  That was the end of 2013, the so-called Megatons to Megawatts Program, 500 tons of HEU blended down into commercial nuclear reactor fuel.  And I just kind of conjured that up, again, because – well, first of all, I think certainly it goes down as one of the absolutely great nonproliferation programs.  But I would just note that in my first go around the track at DOE in the Clinton administration, we had a science-based negotiation to put that deal back on track in 1999.  It had fallen off the rails.  And we did that.  And I just raise that now from the point of view that it was maybe the practice run for science-based negotiation and trying to address some of these critical nuclear materials problems.

Well, OK, so let’s turn to Iran and an update on the JCPOA.  We’re just over two weeks past implementation day, which by definition is the day on which the IAEA certified that Iran had met the conditions laid down in the JCPOA.  In terms of the science-based focus, I would just note that basically, the negotiations clearly were having a hard time making substantial progress.  On the one hand, the negotiation was about a substantial rollback of Iran’s nuclear program in return for the relief of sanctions – of a certain class of sanctions, at least.  So on the Iranian side, the nuclear program was the giver. 

And frankly, I think without having Mr. Salehi head of that program, a former foreign minister and vice president of Iran – without having him directly involved, it was just going to be very, very difficult to define the parameters of the nuclear rollback.  And so it was really in recognition of that, that he and I were put into the negotiation to really focus on the nuclear dimensions and how we would have a substantial rollback, accomplish the fundamental objectives laid down by President Obama, while preserving the essentials, as required by Iran’s supreme leader and President Rouhani. 

So that was really the dynamic that led to this rather unusual situation in which on our side Secretary Kerry and I were negotiating these tracks, and on their side Foreign Minister Zarif and Mr. Salehi.  I want to emphasize – I’ve done this many times – but I never want to forget to say that clearly when Salehi and I were put into the negotiation, the science component received a lot more public attention, but the Department of Energy, its national laboratories, which are an incredible resource of nuclear knowledge, nuclear science, and nuclear technology – I want to make it very clear: they were involved throughout all the negotiations, the entire time, providing the technical underpinning. 

When this got elevated to kind of a direct negotiation that it became more public.  I just want to say that we had seven national laboratories and two additional nuclear sites involved in the negotiation throughout its entirety, providing the analysis.  And they will continue to be involved in this period now of implementation and verification, certainly supporting the IAEA in its jobs.

So let me, again, just bring you up to date.  I think I’ll be saying things which to many of you are familiar, but just to make sure we’re all on the same page in terms of what it is that Iran had to do and did do, with a little extra color, in terms of meeting the JCPOA requirements.  Plutonium, HEU, are the two fundamental materials under discussion.  We sometimes forget because of the tremendous focus on, you know, centrifuges and making HEU, that actually the plutonium pathway is one that really dominated the discussion for quite a long time, the issue of a reactor called Arak, A-R-A-K.  It sounds too much like Iraq. 

The Arak reactor was one of major concern because it was – it would be a – a heavy-water reactor and would be a prolific producer of plutonium. Let’s just say, one to two weapons worth a year.  And there was a lot of concern about that reactor going hot, because, not getting into too much detail, going hot could limit certain options in terms of addressing its plutonium production. 

In this agreement, the plutonium pathway is blocked through a number of measures. Most specifically the calandria or core of that reactor has been pulled out of the reactor and filled with concrete.  It is permanently out of commission. 

It is to be replaced through some international collaboration.  Iran is responsible –Iran has to be the project manager, if you like – and build a replacement reactor.  But they will do so with the help of the international community, and specifically the United States and China are co-chairing a technical working group that will work on verification of design of a new reactor, aimed at meeting certain parameters.  Those parameters include an order of magnitude reduction in plutonium production, to effectively not make it a practical pathway.  It actually will increase the reactor’s capacity for some fundamental research, but the key for us in in nonproliferation is that it is a dramatically plutonium production, way below a significant quantity in its annual production. 

But of course, there are other features as well.  (Background noise, laughter.)  That’s a Geiger counter.  (Laughter, background noise) – I’ll just keep talking. 

MS. HARMAN:  Keep talking.

SEC. MONIZ:  Iran will – is furthermore committed to send the fuel – spent fuel from that reactor out of the country for its lifetime.  It has committed to no additional heavy-water reactors for 15 years, with an additional statement that it is not their intent to actually go back to a heavy-water reactor, although the option is preserved after 15 years.  (Background noise)

I don’t have my cellphone.  They’re in the car.  (Laughs.) 

MS. HARMAN:  Maybe it’s mine.

SEC. MONIZ:  But Jane has her cellphone.

MS. HARMAN:  I know, but it’s off. 

SEC. MONIZ:  (Laughs.) 

MS. HARMAN:  Yeah, that’s – yeah, mine’s off.

SEC. MONIZ:  In addition, they have agreed to cap their heavy water inventory at a level required to service the new reactor design, which as I said is –

(Background noise, off-side conversation.)

SEC. MONIZ:  Well, OK, let’s carry on in any case.  (Laughs.)  So, as I said, there are – so there are all of these components in terms of fundamental redesign of the reactor, spent fuel storage sent out of the country, no additional heavy-water reactors, heavy water limitations.  And I would just say that on the heavy water limitations, there is a certain analogy to that HEU deal the Megatons to Megawatts deal, in the sense that those materials that had been of concern, all of the excess will go into – on commercial terms – go into benign utilization, in research, in industrial processing, in the whole set of uses for heavy water.  So all of that’s been done, as I said.  And the IAEA certainly has verified the steps up to now, including Iran’s sending of its additional surplus heavy water outside of Iran already, not to mention the concrete in the calandria. 

On uranium, HEU, of course, the issue was the two enrichment sites at Natanz and Fordow.  And as you know, they had – they had built up to nearly 20,000 centrifuges, they had a stockpile of about 300 kilograms of roughly 20 percent enriched uranium, and about 12 tons of low-enriched uranium, typically around 4 ½ to 5 percent enrichment.  I think you all know the story that the number of centrifuges that will enrich – and again, I want to emphasize this has been done – they are down to roughly 5,000 – just over 5,000 operating centrifuges.  But I want to add that not only were the other centrifuges removed, so was infrastructure feeding the centrifuges removed. 

And very importantly, and something that does not get, frankly, enough attention, because it is core to the agreement, is a 15-year commitment to no more than 300 kilograms of 3.67 percent enriched uranium.  You may ask, why 3.67 (percent)?  It turns out that is the lowest as a fuel in part of a Bushehr core.  And 300 kilograms is not much more than a kind of a working inventory to maintain their much lower program. 

It, in and of itself, contributes the majority of the increase of the so-called breakout time from just over two months to a minimum of a year in terms of the time it would take Iran, if it chose to breakout, to assemble the high-enriched uranium for a single nuclear explosive.  That restriction on the inventory is absolutely central.  And it alone accomplishes a majority of that extension of the breakout time.  That has been verified by the IAEA. 

And here – I mentioned earlier how we were collaborating with China in terms of the Arak redesign.  I want to emphasize the amount of international – the degree of the nature of international collaboration to accomplish our objectives has been really outstanding.  It goes without saying that this was a P5+1 plus EU negotiation in the first place.  And the coherence of the P5+1 and the EU is, itself, a remarkable story.  It’s not as though there aren’t some differences of opinion among those groups on a variety of issues.  And yet, totally together in terms of negotiating this deal.  Well, I would could argue one little exception, but fundamentally incredible coherence.

But in the implementation, again, we had the China story.  Here in this question of [removing materials from] Iran, in a remarkably short time – and I’ll be straightforward, a significantly shorter time than I thought was going to be possible – reaching the requirements required a lot of cooperation.  In this case, Russia, probably first among equals in terms of that cooperation, in terms of not only removing – again, most of those thousands and thousands of kilograms of low-enriched uranium, removing 20 percent uranium, of removing some scrap materials for which are not a very attractive prospect.  But they put it all on a ship, and they moved it out.  Very big contribution.

But that wasn’t enough.  Without going into all the details, there was – this, again, conjures up the Megatons to Megawatts deal, for the aficionados in the house – there was a natural uranium component that had to be addressed.  Norway stepped up.  Kazakhstan stepped up, Azerbaijan stepped up to all make this thing happen in a very, very compressed timescale.  So, really quite extraordinary.  And again, all of this has happened.

Finally, the covert concerns.  So HEU and plutonium are clearly the pathways, but what I’ve talked about so far is addressing the program that would be remaining as part of a buildup of civilian nuclear capacity.  But obviously the concern is also that there could be a covert activity.  I’m not going to talk about the previous military dimensions.  We can discuss it later if you wish.  But the issue is going forward of what can we do on the covert side. 

I want to emphasize that despite what is said in some quarters, the agreement is unique and breaks new ground in terms of nonproliferation requirements.  One bit of new ground is that Iran – well, Iran is already now into compliance – it is not ratified yet, but into compliance with the additional protocol, which as you know is the basis for access to undeclared sites that are suspicious.  However, for the first time there is a finite time of response to an IAEA request for access to any site for which there are grounds to suspect unallowed activity.  It’s 24 days is the maximum time clock.  We are extremely confident about our ability to detect the use of particular uranium in facilities.  But again, just having the time clock is itself a first.

There’ll be a procurement working group through which Iran must go for its purchases.  There are new – not only quantitatively new but qualitatively new elements, such as a 25-year verification of the uranium supply chain.  There is, for the first time, not only a commitment, which all NPT signatories have, to not pursuing a nuclear weapon, but for the first time, forever, there’s a commitment to not pursuing weaponization activities – things like explosive-driven neutron generators.  You can imagine why that might be useful in one context.  Multipoint initiation of explosives, et cetera.  And for 15 years, no work with uranium metal.

So these are really new steps that have been taken.  And there are others, which are not new in the international context but new in this context – for example, allowing the IAEA to use advanced technologies.  And that’s already happened.  Electronic seals are in place, wired up to central data collection systems for the IAEA.  As you can imagine, the IAEA has been handed and enormous new responsibility, like 25 years of verifying the uranium supply chain.  But these are all in place and moving forward. 

So it’s a pretty remarkable – pretty remarkable story.  And certainly it will take the support of the IAEA in getting that done.  I mentioned that earlier.  That will be financial support.  It will also be the kind of support that we will continue to offer in terms of our – for example, our laboratory capabilities in terms of new technologies and the deployment of technologies.  So finally – so that’s what I think I would stop with in terms of Iran. 

I’ll just say a few words, since Jane [Harman] invited a broader discussion.  On the nuclear materials side, again, the job is not over, and I don’t mean just in Iran.  One thing that might be of interest, and I won’t go into great detail, but last week in the run-up to the nuclear security summit at the end of March, March 31st to April 1st, we held at Lawrence Livermore Laboratory, one of our weapons labs – we held a scenario-based exercise with 37 countries, many at the ministerial level, and key international organizations, like the IAEA, like Interpol, going through a scenario of HEU falling into terrorist hands, and what do you do?

It was very interesting.  One the one hand, we had a pre-meeting in which the delegations had a chance to go around and see some of the technologies at Livermore that we use here.  For example, nuclear forensics, a key issue in this kind of an event.  Needless to say, international capabilities in something like that are highly variable.  So one of the lessons that came out for sure is what kind of international cooperation, how do international organizations work on this, in the case of an event.  But we also had, you know, as these exercises always point out – for example, I’ll stay with forensics.  I’ll talk about the United States.

In a forensics examination, we can get some information very quickly.  But to get the full information takes weeks.  Weeks may be a great laboratory timescale.  It may not be a senior official decisional timescale.  So how do you manage the mismatches between that?  How do you manage public acceptance?  So this was – this was, as I say, a big deal and a remarkable exercise with tremendous participation.  And this is a prelude, as I say, to the nuclear security summit which will be coming up end of March.  And which will continue the issues around the success that we’ve had working with other countries in terms of removing weapons-usable materials from across the board.

Now, that is the end of my remarks, except that you mentioned transactional versus transformational.  And maybe I’ll just conclude with a comment that I believe that is the correct characterization today.  The question is, will it remain that way, which is OK, a lot of progress?  But I also don’t think we should at least – we should blunt our hopes and aspirations that it will grow into something that is transformational in terms of relationships in the region, relationships with us.  And ironically, I would say, the more the implementation of the agreement is transactional, the better chance we have for it to become transformational.  Thank you.  (Applause.)

(END)
