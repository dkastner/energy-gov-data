Secretary Moniz's Remarks at the Schlesinger Medal Ceremony and Energy Security Symposium in Washington D.C. -- As Delivered

October 1, 2014 - 11:49am

Addthis

Dr. Ernest Moniz
Former Secretary of Energy

I wanted to start off by saying that Dan (Poneman) will be ending a distinguished tenure of over five years here at the department, breaking various records.  When I came here last year in May, asked Dan to stay on for at least a year.  He’s hung in there for 16 months.  And we’ll be thanking him, maybe roasting him a little bit too tomorrow, but it’s been great working together.

We’ll take care of that business more tomorrow.  But Dan has – as one would expect, has touched many, many parts of the Department, really all of them.  But for example, as stepping forward in leading the Department in responding to some of the major tragedies that we’ve seen like the American response to Fukushima, like the response to Superstorm Sandy. Anyway, tomorrow we’ll have a much more full accounting.  But I also will mention relevant to today’s discussions that we jointly had the pleasure of having a very informative lunch with Jim Schlesinger not too many months before he passed away.  And so we’ve been a great team, and we had the chance to be jointly informed by Jim and others, again, thanks to Dan.

Also today, happy birthday, DOE.  Today is our 37th birthday, and that’s not a coincidence that we’re having this today on our birthday, because this is when Jim Schlesinger came and stood up the Department on October 1st, 1977.  And of course, we’ll be returning to that.

Secondly, I would just add that yesterday the President put out a proclamation that October is Energy Action Month, so we’re all here also for the first event of Energy Action Month, where we’ll bring more focus to energy security issues, climate change, clean energy issues, and advanced manufacturing for the energy business.  So it will be a busy month.

Let me also thank first the three individuals who were maybe an informal planning group, steering group, ideas group for creating the Schlesinger Award – Charlie Curtis, who you’ll be seeing soon; our dear friend, kind of chaired this informal group that included John Deutch, who you’ll be seeing soon, and John Hamre, who you’ll be seeing soon.  They were very instrumental. I want to just say Charlie was really the key mover in terms of our going to this award, and it was a terrific idea and we’re very pleased to be here.

And Jeff Hughes is here. Jeff became kind of the project executive director and did also a terrific job in terms of assembling this meeting and all the preparations.  Recognizing everyone would lead us to be here for a long time, but I do want to single out our former Appropriations Chair, Senator Bennett Johnston here in the front row. And I think maybe we’ll see Senator Domenici at some point today as well. Certainly Bennett and Pete have been giants, really, in terms of advancing this Department’s agenda, advancing the energy agenda.  Bennett still remains very engaged in that, and we welcome him back to the Department of Energy. 

And finally, here in the front row, I want to recognize, as Dan did, Ann Schlesinger, Jim’s daughter.  And we’ve already started last evening trading some stories, and it’s been great to have Ann here as well.

We’ll start with a story, which again, those here in the front row are all familiar with, but it’s kind of a good indicator of Jim’s drive to get this Department set up. The story is that at one point Jim walked into the Oval Office and – at least the legend goes – with President Carter on the phone, Schlesinger just kind of putting down in front of the President a hand-written note that read, “the real property at 1000 Independence Avenue to be the headquarters of the Department of Energy, yes or no.”  And Carter checked off “yes.” Jim walked out of the office with his new building in hand – this beautiful building in which we are currently.  But then a couple days later, the story goes on, that Secretary of Defense Harold Brown called Schlesinger in a rage, protesting that he had gone over his head and pilfered the home of the Army Corps of Engineers.  And Schlesinger replied, but Harold, you know you would have objected if I had phoned you. 

We also had lunch with Harold Brown recently here, and Harold has not forgotten.  He made that very, very, very clear. 

So 37 years ago Jim Schlesinger accomplished the move here, established a Department, which had been temporarily bivouacked at Jackson Place  And at that time, already about 20,000 employees, budget of about $10 billion.  And the Department, in its formation brought together kind of these two missions that had co-existed within the Federal government in terms of nuclear security activities, including design, construction, testing nuclear weapons at that time, and a rather kind of loose mix of energy-related programs.  And I think the point here is that if you look at Jim Schlesinger’s history and contributions, one can see how people matter in terms of the way this Department is shaped.  I think today it still very much bears the imprint of Jim Schlesinger’s formation of the Department.

He also told the opening day crowd – actually at Jackson Place – that “to resolve our energy problems, the United States will have to go through a transition along with the other nations of the world. Through the effective work of all the people of this Department, we expect to rise to that task.”

Of course, in those years there was the immediate issue of the oil embargoes, the oil crises of the ’70s was an immediate task.  And certainly that led to actions such as designing and standing up the Strategic Petroleum Reserve, which President Ford first signed into law in 1975. 

With that, again, my own anecdote is that my very first introduction to Jim Schlesinger came at a Senate hearing.  It was the very last hearing that I did in the Clinton administration.  I was Undersecretary.  It was not devoid of a certain political atmosphere.  This was in October of 2000, just prior to the election, and Jim had been brought in on a second panel – clearly with the explicit intent of sharply criticizing what we had done in using the petroleum reserve in that period. 

I’m not going to go into the specifics, but just to say that I think it really showed Jim’s character, his analytical approach and his commitment to really learning and understanding what the story was in that as the hearing went on, he clearly was listening. After the hearing, the next day, he called me to have lunch at the Army Navy Club – we had never met before that hearing.  We went through the arguments – he recognized that there were real arguments to be made and, actually, was complimentary of that action at that time.  And I just think that shows that Jim was always interested in and open to a real discussion about issues and if you had a good argument, he’d change his mind, and if you didn’t, he’d roll over you.  That was terrific, I thought. 

But, of course, as this Department was formed, while there was the strong oil driver at that time, the Department did launch programs such as the initial looks at unconventional oil reservoirs in that time period to potentially open up more domestic resource, but, clearly, it was not a “Department of Oil.”  And what we saw was the development of, essentially, the full spectrum of the kinds of activities that we see today as quite relevant to both energy security issues and our clean energy environment economic issues in the sense of looking at renewables, efficiency and the whole spectrum.  So, really, this ground work was laid for addressing our oil dependence on both the supply side and the demand side, in terms of lessening the dependence on oil going forward. 

So the world has changed in many ways, clearly, since that time, but I think the core vision that Jim brought forward in forming the department still pertains – still pertains today.  That’s why we have the Schlesinger Award for Energy Security. 

It’s clearly a tremendously important subject.  In fact, today, the – on the energy security front, we are deeply, of course, engaged in two different areas – one is the whole situation in Europe right now, of course, driven by the Ukraine situation.  And our teams are playing in a very important role as we speak right now, in terms of technical assistance for the short term as well as working with the EU to formulate longer-term plans.  And, of course, we still have the great unrest in the Middle East and the still somewhat unpredictable paths forward.  So energy security is certainly something that we in the Administration are very, very concerned with and there’s no more appropriate person, I think, to have named this award after or vice versa – Jim giving us the motivation to create this focus on energy security. 

Now, in addition, when Jim set up the Department, it was clearly taking the science legacy of the AEC and keeping it certainly within the Department of Energy but also deepening it.  And today, we have a 17 lab national lab system that continues to be at the core of our science and technology enterprise, which is what this Department is all about, but also critical to the science and technology enterprise in the country.  In fact, last year, just the user facilities at these national laboratories served almost 30,000 scientists across the country.  So there’s no doubt that this was embedded in the Department very firmly at its beginning. 

And, of course, on the nuclear security mission, stockpile stewardship continues to be an absolutely core mission.  Jim Schlesinger, as you all know, had a very deep engagement in all the nuclear security issues, certainly when he was at the Department of Defense, and then at Department of Energy.  Today, we are in a different world in the sense of no testing since 1992. But here, the scientific commitment is what is now underpinning our confidence in the stockpile.  And it’s the same scientists, basically, at those laboratories, with their nuclear expertise, that underpin our nonproliferation mission, our mission to control and hopefully eliminate nuclear weapons usable materials.

So the is the thread I’m trying to establish – probably not a surprise to most of you here – is that I think the Department got off on the right foot in terms of its establishing that science and technology foundation.  It established its core missions – sometimes whimsically called weapons and windmills, quarks and quagmires – the quagmires is a more recent, post-Cold War acquisition. But, even there, I would say there are hard problems but we have the tools, we have the people to address it. 

So, with that, I will pivot to the award.  And, probably, you’ve figured out from the program and from the stage that Dan Yergin is our first Schlesinger Energy Security awardee.  The award, again, honors individuals’ distinguished contributions to advancing our understanding of the threats, opportunities and energy policy choices impacting the domestic and international energy security interests of the United States through analysis, policy or practice.  And I might say that another intersection with Jim Schlesinger and John Deutch was a Council on Foreign Relations commission that they co-chaired looking at energy security and oil issues. That was a place, in that working group led by Jim and John, where, in fact, this whole – the concept, really, of energy security as a collective responsibility among allies and friends really emerged very strongly and that today is now part of our energy security posture. 

So, again, Dan Yergin is our first awardee.  Fine scholar, great author and, of course, as I’m sure all of you know, his ability to take the kind of large kind of historic and economic trends, infuse them with a sense of real flesh and blood people who made them happen, risk taking, dramatic confrontations, flashes of genius and strokes of luck, good and bad, I think are all the kinds of things that Dan has captured so brilliantly in his writings.

Clearly, the prize in 1991 laid out an unrivaled history of oil, which reached a broad audience and earned him the Pulitzer Prize.  Then “Commanding Heights” in 1998 traced the rise of free markets. And his most recent major work “The Quest,” which is a very ambitious, thick exploration – think in size – of the past and potential future of the energy system writ broadly.

In noting Dan’s contributions to energy security, in particular, I would just highlight one element which is his emphasis on how functioning markets are, themselves, a way of addressing energy security by minimizing the impacts of disruptions. For example, using Hurricanes Katrina and Rita and the – and the recovery from those as a good example as to how disruptions are mitigated by functioning markets. 

So, with that, I’m going to conclude.  Dan will make some remarks; give his perspective on energy security.  And so I’m going to call Dan forward so that we can exchange the award and then listen to your – to your perspective.
