#SpaceWeek: Science of the Cosmos

During #SpaceWeek we covered the Department of Energy's space expertise, from nuclear-powered spacecraft to lightsabers. (Really.) Here's what you missed.

Storified by Energy Department ·
Tue, Jun 16 2015 14:22:59

Space might not come to mind when you think of the Energy Department, but it should; the Department plays a critical role in America's space missions.

Energy
For example, dozens of spacecraft rely on nuclear power systems developed by National Laboratories. Take a look at this infographic to see how far, far away we've made it!
Infographic: Where in Space is the Energy Department?
In the infographic above, learn about the space missions, past and present, where technology from the Energy Department and its National Labs has made discovery possible. | Infographic by Sarah Gerrity, Energy Department. Ben Dotson It's #SpaceWeek on Energy.gov. Throughout the week, we'll be exploring energy and science on the cosmic frontier.
The same nuclear power systems also make it possible to conduct science experiments on the Moon...

#SpaceWeek continues! Much of what we know about the #moon comes from experiments left behind on its surface by #Apollo missions 12 through 17. These scientific instruments were powered by 70-watt radioisotope thermoelectric generators -- the gray device pictured here -- a type of #nuclear #energy system developed by the Energy Department for use in @NASA missions. Nuclear power sources have made dozens of important #NASA missions possible by converting heat to energy to power #spacecraft, as well as keeping instruments warm enough to work in deep #space. Missions like #Voyager 1 and 2, the #Mars #Curiosity rover and #NewHorizons all rely on nuclear power to keep on ticking as they journey through our #solarsystem and beyond. | Photo courtesy of NASA. #spaceflight #spaceexploration #astronomy #history #Pluto ·

Quick Fix Gives Mars Rover's ChemCam Sharper Vision
Don't you hate it when your photos turn out blurry? Scientists at the Energy Department's Los Alamos National Laboratory do. So when the ultra-precise camera they developed for NASA's Mars Curiosity rover started going fuzzy, they came up with a solution that not only fixed the problem -- it made things clearer than ever.
Back here on Earth, scientists are working on an instrument that will give us the most detailed images of space ever.
