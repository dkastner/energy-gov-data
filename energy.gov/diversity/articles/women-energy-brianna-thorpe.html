Women @ Energy: Brianna Thorpe

July 9, 2015 - 10:25am

Addthis

Brianna Thorpe is a student researcher working with Thomas Jefferson National Laboratory. She worked with Arizona State University’s Meson Physics Group to build the triplet polarimeter currently used in one of the Hall D experiments.

Check out other profiles in the Women @ Energy series and share your favorites on Pinterest.

Brianna Thorpe is a student researcher working with Thomas Jefferson National Laboratory. She worked with Arizona State University’s Meson Physics Group to build the triplet polarimeter currently used in one of the Hall D experiments. This device measures the degree of polarization of the photon beam used in experiments. She is currently working with Jefferson Lab’s Detector Group to help build a gamma camera, a device which is often used in nuclear medical imaging. This will be one of the first handheld, wireless gamma cameras. In the fall of 2015, she will join the DarkLight group to study the nature of dark matter where she will help to design a detector array used in Jefferson Lab’s Free Electron Laser.

Brianna is also the chair of Sundial, an exciting organization of students and faculty at Arizona State which works hard to promote diversity and build a strong, healthy community in physics. She is passionate about science communication and providing underprivileged and at-risk youth with opportunities in physics.

1) What inspired you to work in STEM?

Though I spent most of my school years struggling with math and science, I never lost my curiosity about the universe. With the encouragement of my teachers, I entered Arizona State University’s physics program. Within the first three weeks of my freshman year, I got involved with Thomas Jefferson National Laboratory and have been passionate about nuclear and particle physics ever since.    

If I had to pick just one thing that inspired me to enter and remain in STEM, it would be the support of my friends and family. My mom and my best friend (a fellow nuclear physics researcher) have been my biggest fans from the beginning. They never stop pushing me to explore the universe!  

2) What excites you about your work at the Department of Energy?

There are so many things! The scientists, mentors, and students that I’ve met have had such great attitudes. There’s always something new to explore, another question to be answered, and another way to go about answering that question. Since I began doing research for Jefferson Lab, I’ve never felt like I’m working some 9-5 job. Instead I feel like I’m part of this big adventure full of infinite possibilities.

Most of all, and what I’ve been excited about from day one, has been the sheer power of our research. Particle accelerators are thousands of times as hot as the sun. Matter literally pops in and out of existence in our collisions. We rewrite the laws of nature and challenge our understanding of the physical world. That is mind-blowing! Getting to be part of this, getting to see a side of our universe few even know exists, is the most empowering experience I’ve ever had.

3) How can our country engage more women, girls, and other underrepresented groups in STEM?

Growing up,I enjoyed drawing and writing stories much more than science. I would spend hours painting and drawing, and writing stories. I struggled in my math and science classes. I just couldn’t grasp concepts like division and multiplication; concepts that seemed so simple. Often, I’d hear my teachers and peers say that “girls are better at art and boys are better at math”. At some point, long before high school, I resigned myself to that rule. I could never be good at math because I was girl. That resignation morphed into a strong dislike of any math and science class, and I ultimately swore I would never touch those subjects after high school. I was fortunate enough to have supportive parents and friends who encouraged me to become a scientist, but I know a lot of girls and women who weren’t that lucky.

This idea that certain types of people are just inherently good at certain things is damaging. We push away people who could have been game-changers in their field because we convince them that they have some disadvantage because of their identity. The thing is, though, that the universe doesn’t care if you’re a boy or a girl. The next Higgs Boson isn’t going to only let boys find it. The universe and research is for everyone, and it needs everyone!

If we want to get more underrepresented groups into STEM, we need to change the narrative. Tell them that they can not only be good at, but that they can be anything and everything! Don’t teach them that they were born with certain limitations, but that they develop skills and achieve success through hard work and determination.

4) Do you have tips you'd recommend for someone looking to enter your field of work?

Do everything, especially the things you think you can’t do! There are many opportunities out there for young scientists, and many experienced people who are eager to help you. Don’t let your self-doubt stop you from taking full advantage of that. If you really go all in, you’ll be amazed at what you accomplish.

Also, experimental nuclear and particle physics can be very, very programming-heavy. Whether you’re analyzing terabytes of data or building devices for huge experiments, you’ll end up working with code at some point. Start familiarizing yourself with programming as early as possible. You don’t need to be a whiz at it, but you need to know enough to be comfortable with what you need to do.

Finally, develop public speaking skills. If you’re doing research, you will end up giving poster presentations and talks. If you’re confident in front of an audience you’ll feel better about your performance, your work, and yourself. That confidence is going to make you stand out!

5) When you have free time, what are your hobbies?

A year ago, I fell in love with salsa dancing. What started off as an “easy” elective has turned into my favorite hobby. I’ve become addicted to the freedom and energy of the dance! I dance on my own about eight hours a week and spend another six hours in practice for my two performance teams. The best part, is teaching my fellow physics students how to dance. I love seeing them cut loose, act silly, and have fun. When I’m not dancing, studying, or working in the lab, I model for local designers in Arizona and write poetry.
