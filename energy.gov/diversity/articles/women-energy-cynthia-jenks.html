Women @ Energy: Cynthia Jenks

February 20, 2015 - 10:26am

Addthis

Dr. Cynthia Jenks is the assistant director for Scientific Planning and the Division Director of Chemical and Biological Science at the U.S. Department of Energy’s Ames Laboratory in Ames, Iowa.

Check out other profiles in the Women @ Energy series and share your favorites on Pinterest.

Dr. Cynthia Jenks is the assistant director for Scientific Planning and the Division Director of Chemical and Biological Science at the U.S. Department of Energy’s Ames Laboratory in Ames, Iowa. She received her B.S. in chemical engineering in 1986 from the University of California, Los Angeles. She received a M.S. degree in chemical engineering in 1988 and a M.Phil and Ph.D. in chemistry from Columbia University in 1991 and 1992, respectively.  Cynthia did her postdoctoral work at Iowa State University and the U.S Department of Energy’s Ames Laboratory, joining the scientific staff of the Ames Laboratory in 1995. Her research interests are in the areas of surface structure and reactivity, surface structure-property relationships, catalysis, and thin film growth. She is a Fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science.

What inspired you to work in STEM?

In the beginning I was led to science through my love of math. I also had a really good chemistry teacher in ninth grade. I was excited by being able to imagine how things are moving around and how chemistry is happening-- the entire concept of trying to understand what you can’t see. Despite our technological advances this still remains a challenge in chemistry in a lot of ways.

At the time I was getting my degree there just weren’t that many women in chemistry and so I can’t really say that I had a female role model.  I actually enjoyed being one of the few women. People knew who you were; you weren’t one of the masses. I viewed it as a challenge. Later on in my career Pat Thiel (a scientist at the Ames Laboratory and a Distinguished Professor of chemistry at Iowa State University) inspired me simply by doing her job and doing it well.

What excites you about your work?

Scientific research is like putting together a puzzle. You get all these little pieces of data, and you have to put them together in a way where all the pieces fit and tell a story about what is happening. When one piece doesn’t fit, you know there’s something wrong in your thinking and you have to go back. You have to re-decide what the puzzle is supposed to look like. But when you have it—when you put the last piece in and it all fits—there’s this wonderful “a-ha” moment.  Everything comes together, and everything makes sense. That is a great feeling.

Those same puzzle pieces exist in the managerial aspects of my job. I like to work with people from a wide variety of backgrounds, and at a national laboratory you’re working with chemists and physicists and material scientists and chemical engineers. Getting all those different perspectives is really interesting because everybody comes in with a different idea of what’s actually happening. Bringing together all those pieces, the people and resources, building the environment where great science happens, that’s another way to create those “a-ha” moments.

I have always been the sort of person who made sure things got done; I have persistence and a willingness to look at the big picture. Part of the challenge of a job like mine is managing financial resources, equipment, and people in a way that get you to the goal—making sure scientists’ needs are being met so they can do great science. That is a delicate balancing act. It can involve not taking no for an answer, and it can also mean knowing when to be the one to say no. But if you’re the type of person with that kind of determination and willingness to make sure things happen, that will certainly carry you far in this job.

How can our country engage more women, girls, and other underrepresented groups in STEM?

I have a 14-year-old daughter, and I think adults need to be aware of and intervene in the negative peer pressure young women sometimes get. When their friends say “oh, don’t take that class, it’s too hard,” it’s actively discouraging a girl from challenging herself and pursuing her interests. It’s another way girls get told they can’t or shouldn’t do something.

Competitiveness is a quality that I admit having a hard time embracing in myself, and yet it shouldn’t be considered a negative in women’s achievement. It can give you a laser-like focus on the goals you want to attain, and it forces you to figure out what you need to be doing and what you need to discard to reach those goals.  I would tell any competitive young woman to channel this trait in an appropriate way so that it takes you where you want to go, to be better at your studies, better at your science, better at your career, better at being the kind of person you want to be.

Do you have tips you’d recommend for someone looking to enter your field of work?

It’s important to find a good mentor. It should be someone you can trust and who can advise you on things that may come up in your job. Keep your eyes open for opportunities; mentors may come along in untraditional or unexpected places. Be diligent about nurturing good professional relationships. Science is a lot about people, developing collaborations, and communicating well. More and more, you have to be able to talk about your science to a wide variety of audiences, not just your scientific peers.

Know what your leadership style is, and be true to it. Women are often told they need to adapt their leadership style to some other ideal, when it’s actually okay to let your personality work for you and to be yourself in leadership roles.  Certainly you should be open to advice and critique, but be comfortable with your own strengths and your own unique way of getting to success.

 When you have free time, what are your hobbies?

My free time is spent with my family mostly.  I enjoy helping my family pursue their passions.  I also enjoy cooking and collecting antique maps.
