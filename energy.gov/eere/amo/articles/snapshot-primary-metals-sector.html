Snapshot of Primary Metals Sector

July 13, 2016 - 5:20pm

Addthis

EIA national data shows that energy intensity in the primary metals sector has steadily decreased at an average rate of -15.6% since 1991. Energy consumption decreased by -4% from 1991 to 2010. Electricity consumption remained steady, averaging 505 trillion Btu. Non-electricity consumption increased at an averate rate of 17% from 1991 to 1998 then dropping -17% until 2010. According to census data, the number of employees has decreased by 45% since 1991.

Iron and Steel Sectors Potential Energy Savings

A recent Department of Energy bandwidth study shows potential energy savings opportunities in the U.S. iron and steel manufacturing sectors. Two bandwidths are represented, one being symbolic of energy consumed in six of the most energy intensive processes and the other representing energy savings potential for the U.S. iron and steel industry as a whole. The three energy consumption bands (levels) are measures of potential energy savings: Current Opportunity, R&D Opportunity, and Impractical (thermodynamic limitations). If the best technologies and practices available are used to upgrade the six most energy intensive processes, 197 TBtu of energy could be saved each year. An additional 124 TBtu could be saved through the adoption of applied R&D technologies under development worldwide. When energy savings potential is estimated for the U.S. as a whole, the current energy savings opportunity is 240 TBtu per year and the R&D opportunity increases to 150 TBtu per year.

Aluminum Sector Potential Energy Savings

The amounts of energy used onsite in the major processing operations of the U.S. aluminum manufacturing industry are shown in Figure B from a 2007 Department of Energy bandwidth study showing potential energy savings opportunities in the U.S. aluminum manufacturing sector. The bottom band on each bar shows the theoretical energy requirement, while the top band of each bar shows the energy used above the theoretical minimum. The size of the top band is an indication of how large the opportunity is for energy reduction in that process step. Smelting requires 46% of the total energy consumed in U.S. manufacturing of aluminum. The potential gross U.S. energy savings for smelting and aluminum as a whole is 111.84 kWh (109) and 148.78 kWh (109) per year, respectively. Electricity is required for smelting and accounts for 98% of the energy used in the process.

The Advanced Manufacturing Office (AMO) would like to engage the metals sector on the Technical Assistance and research and development (R&D) side.

AMO map activities provide resources including industrial energy statistics, economic indicators, and a visual portrayal of AMO activities by state and NAICS code. To drill down by NAICS code, choose the interested state and scroll down completely.

Technical Assistance supports the increase in productivity and deployment of energy efficient manufacturing technologies and practices, including strategic energy management and combined heat and power, across American industry.

AMO's R&D projects explore novel energy-efficient, next generation materials and innovative process technologies for both specific industry sectors and a wider range of manufacturing industries.

Advanced Manufacturing Office’s Industrial Technical Assistance Programs

Superior Energy Performance (SEP)
SEP is a certification program that provides facilities with a transparent, globally accepted system for verifying energy performance improvements and management practices.

Better Buildings, Better Plants Program & Challenge (BP)
Better Plants is a voluntary partnership initiative to drive significant energy efficiency improvement across energy intensive companies and organizations. Partner benefits include national recognition, technical support, in-plant trainings, energy saving resources, and other opportunities.

Combined Heat & Power Deployment (CHP)
The CHP program provides facilities and stakeholders with resources necessary to identify CHP market opportunities and supports installation of CHP systems in industrial, commercial, institutional, and other applications. Site specific technical assistance is provided through seven regional CHP Technical Assistance Partnerships (CHP TAPs).

Industrial Assessment Centers (IACS)
DOE has partnered with 24 engineering schools around the country to offer small and medium sized manufacturers no-cost energy and water assessments.

Software Tools & Training
Energy System Tools are provided by AMO to help facilities manage and optimize energy use of critical equipment such as steam and boiler systems, process heating, compressed air, motors, pumps, and fans.

eGuide Energy Management Tool is designed to help organizations from all sectors establish an energy management program that systematically improves organizational energy performance. The eGuide gives step-by-step guidance for energy management program implementation, customized to an organization’s level of familiarity with energy management.
