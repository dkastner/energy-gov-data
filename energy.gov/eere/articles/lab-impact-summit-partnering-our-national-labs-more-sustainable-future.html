Lab Impact Summit: Partnering with Our National Labs for a More Sustainable Future

May 26, 2016 - 4:45pm

Addthis

Former Director, National Lab Impact Initiative

Lab Impact Summit

  • The first-ever Lab Impact Initiative Summit on May 4 convened about 300 clean energy thought leaders from both small and multinational companies, as well as experts from incubators, state utilities, and local and federal government, to celebrate successful partnerships between our national laboratories and industry.
  • Now in its third year, EERE's Lab Impact Initiative is expanding the influence of the Energy Department’s national laboratories and research and development investments by broadening and deepening relationships between our labs and industry.
  • During the summit, Ken Washington of Ford Motor Company; Mark Little, a former top executive at GE; and Michael McQuade of United Technologies Corporation, shared inspiring stories about their companies’ fruitful collaboration with our national labs and the urgent need to launch new, commercially viable clean energy technologies into the market.

As a nation facing the growing challenges of climate change, finite fossil fuel supplies, and protecting our environment for future generations, we must not only commit to investing in clean energy—we should lead the rest of the world in doing so. Therefore, one of the top priorities of the Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy (EERE) is to ensure that we are properly investing in the most promising clean energy technologies, getting the most possible value out of those investments, and ensuring the United States remains a world leader in the clean energy revolution.

It’s with this mission in mind that EERE held the first-ever national lab Impact Initiative Summit on May 4 at our National Renewable Energy Laboratory (NREL) in Golden, Colorado. The summit convened nearly 300 clean energy thought leaders from both small and multinational companies, as well as experts from incubators, state utilities, and local and federal government, to celebrate successful partnerships between our national laboratories and industry—and spawn new and innovative collaborations that are driving America’s clean energy economy forward.

Now in its third year, EERE’s Lab Impact Initiative is expanding the influence of the Energy Department’s national laboratories and our research and development investments by broadening and deepening relationships between our labs and industry—including through initiatives like the Small Business Vouchers pilot and the Technologist in Residence (TIR) program, led by DOE’s Clean Energy Manufacturing Initiative (CEMI).  At the same time, we’re enhancing private-sector access to our world-class lab resources and capabilities for small and large manufacturers so they can innovate in areas like 3D printing (led by Oak Ridge National Laboratory) and high- performance computing (led by Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory).

To that end, the Lab Impact Summit featured inspiring stories about successful partnerships from the likes of Ken Washington of Ford Motor Company, Mark Little, a former top executive at GE, and Michael McQuade of United Technologies Corporation. These  and other energy leaders talked about their companies’ fruitful collaboration with our national labs and the urgent need to launch new, commercially viable clean energy technologies into the market.

Ken Washington discussed a number of collaborative projects in which Ford is currently involved. These included Ford’s effort in the United States Council for Automotive Research’s ongoing fuels working group, which is collaborating with several national labs to research alternative fuels and analyze fuel effects on engine efficiency, emissions, and performance. As Washington put it, “magic happens when national labs and businesses meet in the middle.”

Jon Lauckner of GM also referenced the necessity of partnering with the labs, discussing how Argonne National Laboratory’s work on lithium-ion batteries has helped GM’s advanced battery lab to create the Chevy Volt and Bolt.

These are just a few of the projects that show what we can achieve when our national labs work with external partners of all stripes—small businesses, universities, large entities, and utilities.

Other highlights from the summit included a panel of national lab directors led by NREL’s Dr. Martin Keller, focusing on new contracting mechanisms and other innovative models for forming vital relationships between labs and industry.

At a panel session focused on strengthening our innovation ecosystem, participants learned about plug-and-play options for partnering with the labs. These opportunities for collaboration include Cyclotron Road, which couples top entrepreneurial talent with the world-class tools and expertise of Lawrence Berkeley National Lab, and Lab-Corps, which trains and empowers scientists to learn how to successfully shift their breakthrough discoveries into high-impact, real-world technologies in the private sector.

A major development announced at the Lab Impact Summit was the follow-on round of applications for CEMI’s Technologist-in-Residence (TIR) program, which pairs a senior technology professional from the private sector with a senior national lab researcher to conduct collaborative research. Throughout their collaboration, they will define the scope of their research together, and have access to the resources of all 17 laboratories at their disposal to work on their project.  Click here to learn more about TIR, or apply today-- applications are accepted on a rolling basis.

The Lab Impact Summit highlighted some of the exciting technology coming out of the labs’ partnerships with industry. These included some cutting-edge vehicles, like an electric, Formula-E car and a hydrogen fuel cell Toyota Mira.

Also on display was our Additive Manufacturing Integrated Energy Demonstration Project. This 3D-printed house and utility vehicle share power using an integrated energy system - changing the way we think about generating, storing, and using electrical power. Utilizing advanced manufacturing and rapid innovation, it remarkably took only one year to go from concept to launch. You can see a video here about this project that explains what it means for the field of advanced manufacturing and how 3D printing is changing all of our clean energy technologies.

In addition to dynamic exhibits, the Lab Impact Summit featured several incredible videos that illustrate some of the dramatic successes stemming from enhanced relationships between our labs and external partners.  Participants left the summit with a comprehensive guide on how to work with our labs as we build new roads to commercialization.

As we make big strides to build a more commercially aware and entrepreneurial culture throughout the lab network, we asked that everyone at the event “hug a lab.”  I know I saw some actual hugs exchanged! Whether you were able to join us in body or just in spirit, I encourage you to visit our summit website or email us at EERELabImpact@ee.doe.gov to learn more about how we can create a stronger and more prosperous America, powered by clean energy.  And don’t forget to hug a lab today!
