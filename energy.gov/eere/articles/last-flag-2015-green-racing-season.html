Last Flag of the 2015 Green Racing Season

October 15, 2015 - 2:30pm

Addthis

A Porsche 911 GT being chased by a Chevrolet Corvette GT under the rain during the last hour of Petit Le Mans 2015 at Road Atlanta. | Photo courtesy of New West Technologies

A Porsche 911 GT closing in on a Mazda Prototype half way through the Petit Le Mans 2015 at Road Atlanta. | Photo courtesy of New West Technologies

Racing fans learning while playing the Green Racing Simulator at Road Atlanta. | Photo courtesy of New West Technologies

Nay Chehab
Program Analyst, Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy

The Petit Le Mans, a 10-hour endurance racecar competition held annually at the beginning of October, is known for being difficult, long, and (since 2006) “green.” It is also the culmination of the annual International Motor Sports Association’s (IMSA) TUDOR United SportsCar Championship series, which determines the winner of the Green Racing Cup.

Supported by the U.S. Department of Energy, U.S. Environmental Protection Agency, and SAE International, Green Racing recognizes racecar teams that go the farthest and fastest with the smallest environmental footprint. Together they use the racetrack to promote, develop, and test cleaner fuels and more efficient vehicle technologies that manufacturers can transfer to consumer vehicles. Green Racing scores are based on vehicle efficiency, the oil used, the amount of greenhouse gases emitted, and the time in which cars complete the race. Much like golf, the lower the score, the better.

The 2015 Petit Le Mans championship race was held on October 3 at Road Atlanta in Braselton, Georgia, and was one of the most gripping events of the year—until a torrential downpour caused officials to stop the race at the 7th hour and 51st minute. Several crashes occurred because of continuous rain, some resulting in complete vehicle wreckage. In the end, the first vehicle to cross the finish line was a Porsche GT Le Mans (GTLM). 

As much as the action kept viewers on the edge of their muddy seats, Green Racing fans were more captivated by the “race within the race.” Given the weather conditions, drivers were more cautious than usual, and the race was “under a yellow flag” multiple times (with a pace car leading the race more slowly than cars would go normally). These conditions resulted in exceptional efficiency, as cars generally become less fuel efficient the faster they drive above 50 miles per hour. While the Porsche GTLM crossed the finish line first, it was not the most efficient. Instead, the Green Racing winner, BMW Team RLL, as well as the other top five Green Racing finishers emerged with unprecedented negative scores.

At the end of the Green Racing season, IMSA compiles the Green Challenge scores and awards the Green Racing Cup to a single team. At the TUDOR Night of Champions awards ceremony, EERE Deputy Assistant Secretary for Transportation Reuben Sarkar presented the Green Racing trophy to this season’s winner, BMW.

The ultimate goal of Green Racing is to promote and test innovative vehicle technologies that can transform the current state of transportation. The racetrack not only provides great public visibility to advanced vehicle technologies but also a laboratory in which cars undergo rigorous and arduous conditions. Technology success on the track can pave the way to its use in cars on our neighborhood streets and highways.

After this season of compelling and environmentally-friendly racing, the Green Racing team looks forward to next year. For more on Green Racing, please visit greenracingcup.org.
