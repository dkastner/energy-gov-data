Meet Johanna Wolfson: New Tech-to-Market Director Dedicated to Moving EERE Innovation into the Marketplace

March 10, 2016 - 4:04pm

Addthis

Dr. Johanna Wolfson, new director of the Technology-to-Market program, brings vision and experience to EERE.

Sharon Cosgrove
Lead Communications Specialist, EERE Communications

Snapshot: Dr. Johanna Wolfson

  • Dr. Johanna Wolfson, EERE’s new director of the Tech-to-Market program, brings expertise in applied research from MIT and the private sector, and success with cleantech startups and the larger question of innovation strategy.
  • The Tech–to-Market program supports a number of initiatives that address financial and technological barriers to market adoption of clean tech innovation.
  • Wolfson is committed to orchestrating success in tech transition by helping startups identify and engage with key stakeholders – industry customers, development resources, and investors – much earlier in the process to help innovators incorporate this vital feedback in building their products.

Dr. Johanna Wolfson has big plans for EERE’s Technology-to-Market program.

Wolfson – the new director of the office's effort to address the barriers to bringing new innovative technologies to market – boasts extensive experience developing pathways to commercialization for cleantech startups, both in academia and the private sector. Wolfson earned her Ph.D. in physical chemistry from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) before furthering her career at Fraunhofer – a global leader in applied research.

Over coffee, Wolfson shared her vision for unifying the sweep of programs dedicated to moving EERE innovations into the marketplace. 

Q: HOW DID YOUR BACKGROUND LEAD YOU TO THIS NEW ROLE?

While in the lab at MIT doing photo-physics research, I was simultaneously learning everything I could about innovation strategy – how stakeholders in the ecosystem affect technology adoption, and how different countries bring technologies to market. And I started to realize that, as a technologist, I wanted my role to be more focused on the whole picture.

At Fraunhofer, I worked with individual startups to validate their new energy technologies in the field, making them less risky for investors and first customers. And at the same time, we were building an ecosystem and funding model that enabled both investors and industry to pay for this early startup work and opening a contract research and development lab for small startups to streamline the process. My background enables me to identify new mechanisms to bring technologies to market, and to facilitate this process by identifying and engaging the right partners to make it happen.

Q: WHAT ARE YOUR PRIORITIES AS DIRECTOR OF TECH-TO-MARKET?

Existing Tech-to-Market programs have demonstrated great impact. I’m excited to weave these programs together to form a more streamlined support structure for clean energy technology development, and to identify and fill gaps not currently being addressed.

The innovation community tends to think that if we give technology developers a little more gas, they might be able to corral the right resources to reach the market. But what we don’t do is to look at the whole picture. How can we most effectively build bridges – from industry customers, development resources, and funders – back to the technology developer? I want us to focus on this bridge-building to get early industry feedback to the innovator and provide them with the expertise and resources to shape their products in response to that feedback. Then I want to demonstrate to the financial community that a robust, supportive ecosystem is in place to guide their investments.

If we engage these stakeholders more strategically, we can actually narrow the valley of death – not just launch technology developers over it.

Q: WHAT ARE WE DOING RIGHT?

Many different programs and organizations have built solutions to pieces of this puzzle. Part of our role can be to help integrate successful approaches to form a better landscape for clean energy innovators. 

In Tech-to-Market, we have terrific programs to launch innovators out of universities and national labs, and to support early-stage companies and the broader energy innovation ecosystem. The Cleantech University Prize competition gets university entrepreneurs started with their businesses, and Lab-Corps is connecting innovators at DOE labs to the voice of industry. Cyclotron Road – in collaboration with our Advanced Manufacturing Office – embeds entrepreneurs within the world-class capabilities at our labs to build their products and companies. Our National Incubator Initiative for Clean Energy supports the organizations that provide critical space and services to innovators. Small Business Innovation Research grants and Small Business Vouchers – enabling innovators to access lab expertise for collaborative research – provide critical non-dilutive funding and services en route to obtaining private investment.

Q: WHAT CHALLENGES DO YOU FACE?

Our Tech-to-Market initiatives represent small dollars in the grand scheme of government investment, and it forces us to be intensely strategic about where the funds go. In contrast to other tech to market efforts that focus project by project, our approach is to help orchestrate a better innovation ecosystem, which requires significant stakeholder engagement and coordination.

Another challenge we face is being responsive to the very nimble, fast-paced innovation community. When you’re a startup, every month matters. You don’t have eight months to figure out a contract: you’ll be dead. It’s part of the reason that small businesses don’t engage more often with the world-class resources at our labs, and why some government funding opportunities just don’t work for startups. If we’re serious about helping innovators, we need to take a hard look at our own processes and ask if we are serving that community in the best way.

We can look back at clean tech success stories and see how industry partners, financial backers, and physical resources were critical to their success. The question is, can we orchestrate that success? Not to the point where we’re picking winners and losers, but so that if a company fails, it’s for the right reasons – because their product didn’t work or there wasn’t a customer – not because they didn’t have the right information or the testing equipment they needed. We can do better at giving the best ideas the best chance of success. That’s my mission.

Watch for more on the Tech-to-Market topic in an upcoming issue of Amped Up! Magazine.
