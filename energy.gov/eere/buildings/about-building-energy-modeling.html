About Building Energy Modeling

Building energy modeling (BEM)—physics-based calculation of building energy consumption—is a multi-use tool for building energy efficiency. Established use cases include design of new buildings and deep retrofits, development of whole-building energy-efficiency codes and standards (e.g., ASHRAE 90.1) and performance-path compliance with those codes (e.g., ASHRAE 90.1 “Appendix G” Performance Rating Method), beyond-code physical asset rating and labeling (e.g., USGBC’s LEED energy credit), and development of prescriptive design guides (e.g., ASHRAE 50% beyond 90.1 Advanced Design and Retrofit Guides). Emerging uses include design of low-energy building control algorithms, continuous commissioning of building mechanical systems, and dynamic building control for energy optimization or demand response. DOE has been actively involved in BEM research and development since the early 1970’s, before its ascension to a cabinet-level department. BTO’s current BEM portfolio is active in six major areas:

  1. It develops the EnergyPlus™ whole-building energy simulation engine. EnergyPlus is a state-of-the-art program capable of modeling advanced low-energy designs and systems. EnergyPlus analysis underlies energy-efficiency codes including ASHRAE 90.1 and the software itself is the basis for a large and growing number of both public and commercial products. EnergyPlus is the only energy simulation engine provided under a commercial-friendly open-source license.
     
  2. It develops the OpenStudio™ software-development kit (SDK) and demonstration application, with significant cost-sharing from utilities and other public and private organizations,. The OpenStudio SDK provides common abstractions and functions that dramatically reduce the effort required to develop applications that use EnergyPlus for energy analysis. The OpenStudio application demonstrates the power of the SDK and is a usable model development workflow in its own right. OpenStudio has been instrumental in accelerating the adoption of EnergyPlus. Many of the recently-developed EnergyPlus applications including DOE’s Asset Score, the California Energy Commission’s CBECC-Com, and Xcel Energy’s EDAPT have been developed using OpenStudio. Like EnergyPlus, OpenStudio is distributed under a commercial-friendly open-source license.
     
  3. It supports testing and validation of energy modeling programs. BTO supports the continual maintenance and expansion of ASHRAE Standard 140, a framework and methodology for testing building energy modeling software using a combination of analytical, comparative, and empirical tests. In Fiscal year 2016, it is embarking on a three-year project to use the new commercial building test facilities at LBNL (FLEXLab) and ORNL (Flexible Research Platform) to define and execute experiments that will generate high-fidelity high-resolution empirical data sets that can be used to validate selected energy modeling calculations against ground truth.
     
  4. Via competitive solicitations and small business awards it funds research, advanced development, and deployment of BEM technologies. One currently active project is the development of a hybrid modeling algorithm that can use zone temperature data streams in lieu of difficult-to-obtain inputs for infiltration and internal thermal mass. A second project looks to develop black-box models for advanced roof-top units.
     
  5. It partners with organizations like the American Society of Heating, Refrigeration, and Air Conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE), the International Building Performance Simulation Association (IBPSA), and the American Institute of Architects (AIA) to support the energy modeling community. BTO sponsors hackathons and modeling competitions as well as student and young-professional travel to IBPSA SimBuild and BuildingSim and the ASHRAE Energy Modeling Conference. It also develops online resources for modelers. Recent projects include the UnmetHours question-and-answer site, the new building energy software tools directory, and the AIA’s 2030 Commitment Design Data Exchange reporting site.
     
  6. It provides technical guidance and support for BEM projects in other BTO programs. These include the Commercial Building Integration program’s Commercial Building Asset Score, the Residential Building Integration program’s Home Energy Score and BeOpt, the Building Energy Codes program’s COMCheck, and BTO’s Strategic Prioritization Tool. It also provides technical guidance and monitoring for BEM projects in BTO’s international collaborations such as the U.S.-China Clean Energy Research Center (CERC) and the U.S.-India Centre for Building Energy Research and Development (CBERD).
