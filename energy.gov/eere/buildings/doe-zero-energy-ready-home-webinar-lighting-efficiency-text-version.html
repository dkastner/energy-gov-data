First slide:
So as we get started here today, I just want to, for perspective, look at the DOE Zero Energy Ready Home specs. And as the name implies, the goal of this program and the homes and builders that we work with is to get homes to that place where they're zero energy ready: With a reasonably sized renewable system, they can get into that neighborhood of zero energy performance. Along those lines, we want to diminish and reduce the loads in the home, and lighting and appliances are a big part of that. As we work on the heating and cooling, water heating loads, lighting and appliances become more and more evident as an area to focus, to continue to reduce loads to reasonable and manageable levels. So under the specs, we have a set of mandatory requirements. And the one which deals with lighting looks for 80 percent of the lighting fixtures to be ENERGY STAR®-qualified, or the lamps themselves to be ENERGY STAR-qualified in 80 percent of the sockets. So a couple things to note about that spec. First off, it doesn't require LEDs. It simply requires ENERGY STAR-qualified lighting, which doesn't necessarily have to be LEDs. And secondly, we're not just quoting like a lumens per watt kind of efficiency spec; we're looking to the ENERGY STAR label. And that's notable because one of the main themes of the DOE Zero Energy Ready Home program is that it definitely involves high-efficiency, energy-efficient homes that can get to zero with some renewables. But it also -- you know, a really key pillar is the performance of these homes. These are high-performance homes that are energy efficient. So they have good indoor air quality. They have good moisture management. They have good comfort for the residents. And hopefully the lighting also follows suit, that it's not just high-efficiency, but the performance is also up to par and leaves residents satisfied and happy with their indoor lighting environment. So that's part of the reason that the program points to the ENERGY STAR specifications for high-efficiency lighting.

So Brooke and David respectively will tackle those topics. And if we do them well and do them right, at the end of the day, the user experience really should be different. Brooke Silber:

And there's never been a time where this is more true than working with LEDs.

Next slide:
LEDs have definitely made our life more complicated. So when asked to do this, I looked at, well, what's going to be helpful? So I thought, well, let me think about how we determine when and where and which LEDs we use. And you kind of see that you start to think about -- oh, all those energy codes. I've got state codes; I've got local codes. I've got the inspectors. I've got my client asking me, do I do a payback analysis? Next slide:

If we go with the idea here today that we're either being asked to do it from an energy standpoint or we're choosing to do it from an energy standpoint, so we're going to deal with all the codes and so forth. And sometimes they know why they've requested it, and sometimes they're not sure; it's just because of the energy, or because it's new, or they just want to know. So often we'll start with maybe looking at a payback analysis for them.

Next slide:

So if I used an MR-16 pipe housing, and I used something that's sort of midrange -- not the low range, not the high range, because we all know we can get prices all over the board, even for a simple MR-16 with a socket downlight -- we might have a fixture, DN fixture cost of $180. And we have a distributor markup, and we've got electrical contract markup. Does seem reasonable. And then let's say we have the cost of the lamp. In this case, I'm going to use it as if we were using a MR-16 LED retrofit lamp. And I'm comparing this against an integral LED. So the integral LEDs are going to have quite a bit more initial cost. And of course, that's carried through with the distributor markup and the electrical con markup. Now I don't have a lamp, but I still end up with a cost differential of those total number of fixtures of not quite double. So if I stayed with, and this is what often some people do, is they're like, well, what happens if I just want to do the electric fences, but I still want to do LEDs? That's why we look at the expenses of LED retrofit. Then the question is, well, the lamp is really expensive; what kind of a savings am I going to have on energy? So that's the next analysis we might run for them. And this is looking at the same project and analyzing it against the halogen, the MR-16 LED replacement, and an integral module. Now, a halogen, we know we can go all the way up to a 50-watt lamp, but even today that's not worth it. We usually stay with a 37-watt or under. So I've based our calculations on that. With a $220 lamp, that gives us a total load effect of 8,140 watts. And if I'm operating that at most eight hours a day -- you have to remember, a lot of us are gone during the day, and at work and doing other things, so we don't tend to operate every room in eight hours a day, so this is absolutely the high end of the spectrum looking at the hours that you're in the house and using it. Let's say that this is a weekend day. And then we look at the MR-16 replacement lamp, and at about 10.5 watts, and I used an integral module. They come in slightly varying wattages, but I used a fairly typical 13 watts to run this analysis. And in running this analysis, what we see is that there is about a savings of somewhere around $15, $16 a day, by using the MR-16 replacement lamp. And that gets us -- if we're only looking at LED replacement lamps -- that gets us an anticipated payback cycle of 1.4 years. So doing that lamp may be very much worth your client's time and money. However, using an integral module gives you 8.8 years. So at that point, you really ask the owner, the client, is this worth it? Or you ask yourself, as a builder, is this worth that energy? And sometimes it definitely may be.

Next slide:
After looking at whether we want to consider an LED, then we start and look at the applications in which LEDs really enhance our lives, enhance our choices, versus sometimes where we may not want to make the choice of an LED.

Next slide:
So, first thing to establish those various areas, those various applications, is to establish a project lighting objective with the owner. It's about getting to know the owner. It's about -- I mean, sometimes you're asking incredibly personal questions on some of these things, such as, how do you shave your legs? Do you shave it in the shower? Do you shave it out of the shower? Or, do you guys read in bed at night? Do you watch television in bed at night? Those are some of the questions that you start to ask people. You're really literally in their life to try and understand how they use their home. And those can help determine some of your objectives.

Next slide:
Within that, you're always considering things like visibility. The whole -- how everything is put together in the house. How things relate to one another. Do the color temperatures match? Do the intensities feel correct and balanced? And that gets you into sort of a composition of brights and relationships between certain surfaces and objects. We're always looking to understand the style or the character of the owners' aesthetic, such as contemporary or modern and minimalist, or traditional. And a lot of times, what you get is people who say, well, traditional, I can't use an LED; only the modern ones do. And that's absolutely not true. There's a lot of things that we can work with them to help achieve that desired aesthetic. And of course, mood has a lot to do with how people feel. You want to feel good when you go home. You want to feel relaxed in certain rooms. Maybe you want to feel energetic when you're sitting in your exercise room. So those are things that we try and make sure that we're listening to, talking about, in terms of how we're going to approach the LED option.

Next slide:
To make sure that we get through today, I didn't list into everything. So I started to look and say, OK, well, what are really good applications that I often use LEDs for in a residence? Under-cabinet lighting or in-cabinet lighting is a wonderful way of introducing LED. There are so many sources today that are nice and small, and there's a lot of great optics to give it a frosted optic. So we no longer see the individual dots, which is not usually desirable. We usually want to see something that's very soft and uniform across the entire surface. Particularly if you're doing an under-cabinet light in a kitchen where you've got tile, which is specular, it's nicer to have something that doesn't give you all of those little pinpoints of light. Some people are probably sitting out there going, I never put LED cabinet lighting in my house. One of the reasons we often do put cabinet lighting in our house is to get vertical surfaces lit. It's amazing: What we're looking at in a house is the vertical surfaces. We often think of lighting only at task surface -- a desk, the kitchen island. But the thing that makes a space feel bright is when we have light on the vertical surfaces: on the finishes, on the books, on the artwork. And so this is an excellent way of getting that visual brightness, which then allows us to use less downlighting and therefore saving energy by putting the energy on a vertical rather than in a task-type situation.

Next slide:
Another good application for LEDs includes things like cove lights and uplighting. When we light up a ceiling, we can make a space feel much brighter than if we're just downlighting the furniture. As I said before about vertical, the same holds true when we uplight a ceiling. It can make the space feel brighter without introducing more energy. Sometimes it seems like a luxury and only in high-end homes are we going to do this, but that's actually kind of a misnomer. A lot of times, what we'll do is we'll look at doing a wallslot along a wall, which again gives us a lot of vertical illumination. And as you can see in this, we have no downlight in that ceiling. We're only going by a wallslot, which is actually giving us less energy in that hallway than a traditional downlighting method.

Next slide:
Other great applications for LEDs include things like step lighting and task lighting, where we're putting the light where we need it when we need it, instead of again, just a general illumination approach. The nice thing about LEDs is these are much smaller sources now. We can have these tiny little step lights, and we can have less obtrusive task lighting, whether it's for our couch or our table or our desk.

Next slide:
So there are some applications that I still think twice about, at least for now, in introducing LED. And that's actually in some of the general lighting or in the downlighting effects. And the reason why is not because I don't want to think about, hey, could I have all of my entire house LED and energy efficient? But it's because I think about the use of that room. So we think about a dining room. Most of us don't eat in our dining room every day. We're either grabbing a bite at our kitchen island, or sitting at our kitchen table, or even if it's beautiful out, we're sitting out on the porch or barbecuing outside. Our dining room is often reserved for those moments when it's a special occasion. And we all want to look good. And we want our food to look good. And so, since we don't use it very often, there's nothing wrong with saying, hmm, I might leave this in an incandescent so I can get that really warm din, which, while LEDs are allowing us to start with warm din, if you've ever seen it, it's not exactly the same quality. So we look for things like quality. Another good example of when we may not use LED is when we're trying to highlight or pinpoint something like a sculpture or, in this case, a plant, a centerpiece. Some of the LEDs just don't get tight enough. The tightest LED now that we've really seen that's usable is something about a 10-degree. To do a focal point like this, we may use something like a 4-degree beam spread, particularly if it's a higher ceiling. So these are just some of the reasons where I start to evaluate the usefulness over the aesthetics or the use of the room associated with LEDs. One of the things we have to remember about energy is it's also times time. So if I'm not using it in a dining room as much, I say you rethink it.

Next slide:
Another time that I really think hard about using LEDs is in decorative lighting. There's a certain quality that we kind of associate with certain kinds of decoratives like candelabras. And we are getting closer and closer and closer with a lot of the LED options out there, but there are still times when you sit there and go, gosh, I just wish it was incandescent, my old favorite. I had a friend and actually a client who said to me, Brooke, do I really have to, if I want to be energy-conscious, put LEDs in my bedside table? And I said, well, no, we don't have to. We can use your standard three-way that you like a lot. Oh, I would just feel so much better, because I could just use that when I read at night. And I just like that option better. So for her room, what we did is we kept the table lamps as incandescent, and we took the tract lighting to an LED retrofit lamp to highlight the photography in her bedroom. Here's another example of when we really thought about it, and we said, huh, in this case, we could do this with LEDs. Some people don't feel that the LEDs give that really, really warm light, like an oil lamp. This was a historic restoration project. And it has a chandelier that had been originally oil. So we looked at using LEDs in a way where we could replicate that feel of oil. So we used a really warm, amber LED to achieve that look with an LED A-lamp type that gave out light in a more general sense. So we had sort of a two-level lighting approach.

So I'm going to jump from there into talking about color, partially because we left off with that amber, and I think that this is really important.

Next slide:
When we think about color in an LED, we remember that when it first started to come out, it wasn't so good. And we are getting much, much better in our color consistency. But we still see an issue. Next slide:

We know we'll get there. We're getting better and better every day.

Next slide:
But let's take a step back and talk about CCT. It's correlated color temperature. It relates, what does that source look like compared to this piece of metal that we put into a black body radiator? We heat it up, and to look at when it's heated to a certain temperature, what does it look like? And that's how we evaluate the lamp. But there's another way that we need to think about how LEDs and other technologies work. It's called spectral power distribution. We all have seen the rainbow. We all know that all the colors of the rainbow make up white light. And we can make the same color temperature with very different levels of different parts of those wavelengths. So if you see these two drawings here -- or, charts here -- what you see is two different sources with the same correlated color temperature. But I can tell you what you're going to see visually is very different. One is going to render things in the blue region maybe a little bit better. You may see a little bit more of a blue undertone in all the colors that you're looking at, versus this one, that's more in the amber range, similar to the one that I was showing you. If you really had a spectral power distribution of that oil lamp, you would see almost all of it up in this region. The thing to think about with LEDs is that they are a limited wavelength source. They have a very narrow wavelength in which we have been trying to make it, spill it out more and more. And again, we are getting better and better at this. But it doesn't mean that one looks exactly like the other. So you need to know, and ask questions of the manufacturers if you've got something specific.

Next slide:
What we often tell our clients is there's nothing better than a visual inspection. A mockup. For instance, in this home, the under-cabinet tile has a pinky and an orange color to it. Next slide:

Next slide:
Alright. Enough about color for now. I'm sure David's going to talk a little bit more about it later. So let's talk about photometrics and how do we evaluate the photometrics. You know, the ENERGY STAR label is good at putting a lot of the information on there.

Next slide:
One of the things they talk about is the output, or the lumen. Lumens refers to the quantity of light in all directions. So if we think of our old friend the A-lamp, we see what's called the cosign distribution, light in all directions. And this is exactly how LED manufacturers kind of measure the efficacy of their LEDs, in lumens. But, one of the things to think about is that an LED doesn't always have a perfect distribution. We've got the substrate on the back, and a lot of them are actually about 180- or maybe even 120-degree output not all directions.

Next slide:
So one of the other measurements or metrics that is really critical for us to look at is what's called center beam candle power. And this is especially true when you're looking at a recessed downlight that would either have an MR-16 replacement lamp -- and so you're trying to evaluate the replacement lamp -- or an integral module where there is a reflector, which helps push the light out and increase the efficiency of that lamp. Well, then what you start to look at is not the lumens. The lumens are not as important as what's called the center beam candle power, which is measured in candellas. So we looked with down here. And we also have -- it falls off at the angle, which is called the field. And I'm going to give you a picture here.

Next slide:
So this portion right in the center -- let me get my cursor up there for you -- this part right in the center is the center beam. This is the center beam. And this is what's called the center beam area. And note we have the area outside it, which is the fill, or the field. And that light can be as important as the center beam, depending on what you're trying to do. LEDs also have a fair amount of spill that come outside, even the field angle, although some are pretty good at controlling the amount of spill nowadays.

Next slide:
So LEDs produce light differently. When we look at something like a halogen, we have a little small tiny filament that's in there, that's reflecting off of the faceted reflector. And it's pushed to a single point to optimize its beam angle, whether it's a 24-degree beam angle, a flood like a 40-degree beam angle, or whether it's a spot, which gets then in some cases to like a 4-degree beam angle. And then what we have in some cases, so there you can see what we have is the beam angle and the field. But an LED produces -- is a LED chip surface. Sometimes those chips are as if it is a single LED with a phosphor over it. And sometimes it's actually a multi-array LED, which will give you multiple shadows. And so it's not optimized necessarily for the reflector that it's in. The reflectors are getting better and better, to looking at it. But no matter what you do, it's a wider surface than the halogen filament. So sometimes you get things like additional spill that we've controlled a little bit better, at this point in time, with a halogen. I'm not saying that it won't change, because we see changes in LEDs constantly.

Next slide:
So looking at a similar study that we did: This is actually looking at various integral modules. We can see that not all the beams look the same. Even though every one of these is advertised as a flood, that flood could be a 35-degree. It could be a 40-degree. But what we see here is they don't look the same at all. This one has a bright spot in the middle. This one has a real soft beam angle with the field as a little bit more uniform to the center beam. This -- the bottom center. Here what we see is a larger center beam with a little bit of spill. And this one, we see almost all softness with a lot of spill happening. So honestly, the only way we know any of this is by evaluating it. So you have to look at what you're offering to your clients or what you're building into your home.

Next slide:
And some people are like, well, does it really make a difference? Is my client going to be able to see it? So I'm giving you an example. Here it happens to be an outdoor dining space. But you can see the bright spot on the table. You can also look at the very center of the picture is a sculpture. This is lit with incandescent light, and then in 2013, we went back and we retrofitted it and changed it to LED.

Next slide:
And the difference is in my opinion fairly dramatic. The table is so much more soft. We don't have the punch on the table. We don't have the same punch down at the stairs.
