Next slide:
So I guess I want to start with exactly what is the WaterSense Labeled New Homes. The WaterSense New Homes program is the first national new home label for water effiiciency. We started labeling homes really in 2010. I think we finalized the specification in 2009. But a few years ago, to be honest, certainly the single attribute programs such as ENERGY STAR® really only focused on things like energy efficiency. Even the multi-attribute programs, things like LEED, or the National Green Building Certification, at that point, to be honest, were a little bit weak on water efficiency. When you consider what an important resource water is and how much of our infrastructure as a nation is dedicated to supplying clean, healthy, safe water, it's a little bit -- well, I don't want to say it's surprising, but it was something that really needed to change. And that's part of what the WaterSense Labeled Homes program is here to do. So, knowing that that was the landscape we were dealing with, we designed the program to work really well and as a relatively easy addition to existing programs such as ENERGY STAR or in this case, like the Zero Energy Ready Homes program. WaterSense Labeled New Homes, our goal is to save at least 20 percent compared to a conventional new home. We also have requirements in there that help educate homeowners. They can continue the water efficiency behaviors. And like all WaterSense labeled products, WaterSense Labeled Homes are third-party certified. So just how much value is in a WaterSense Labeled Home? Well, considering that most of the requirements of WaterSense Labeled Homes are really low-cost, no-cost, relatively easy incremental steps, we think there's a tremendous value there. A family of four living in a WaterSense Labeled Home, compared to a typical new home, is going to save about 50,000 gallons of water a year, and usually more than $600 on their water, sewer, and energy bills. In addition to that, outside, where we do have requirements for landscape design and if installed, irrigation as well, those landscapes are going to be easy to maintain and offer longtime curb appeal. So like I said, it really is a tremendous value.

Next slide:
Now why this is important to people in the building industry is -- and you heard Jamie touch on this, as well -- first and foremost, we think the whole house building science approach really does need to include water. Part of what we're doing in a lot of these programs but also in Zero Energy Ready Homes, is, yes, we're building houses that are going to save money and they're going to be efficient, but at the end of the day, they're simply better homes. When we talk about home buyers, I think most of us would agree that 99 times out of 100, the things that people are first and foremost concerned with are price, location, and style. We don't know what No. 4 is going to be. There's a growing segment of the population for whom the environmental aspect is really important. They want to feel good about their homes. There's people who respond just simply to the convenience factor. of knowing that they're going to be comfortable in their home. And there's people that just simply respond to knowing that better is better. That the home is built with conscious attention paid to the attributes that are going to have a long- lasting impact on what it's like to live and use and be in that home long-term. So the great thing about the whole house building science approach is that it really helps us speak to all of those consumers. And certainly I thinnk WaterSense is a great way to continue that from some of the areas that we've already been looking at that, at the whole house building science approach, and add even more convenience, efficiency, and confidence in the home. So that's what a WaterSense Labeled Home is.

Next slide:
Let me take a quick step back and talk about why is WaterSense here, and why does EPA, the agency that I work for, feel that a program like WaterSense was a good way to approach some of the challenges that we faced in the country.

Jamie Lyons:
Jonah, real quick? I had a timely question, since you're describing WaterSense homes, what they are. Can existing homes be WaterSense labeled?

Jonah Schein:
Existing homes can be WaterSense labeled. The program is designed with new construction in mind. But it can be applied to rehab projects. There are some things that tend to be a little challenging with rehab projects, and we don't adjust the criteria. So, when we get to talking about the hot water distribution later, which very much has to do with how the pipes are laid out inside the home, we don't adjust that for existing homes. So you have to meet the performance requirements, even though it might be a pretty heavy lift to open up the walls and rearrange the plumbing. There are some potential work-arounds for that, but that's one of the things that tends to make existing homes a little bit of a heavier lift when it comes to the WaterSense label. Does that answer the question?

Jamie Lyons:
That's perfect; thank-you.

Next slide:
Getting back to why WaterSense is here. First of all, we need more water in this country, all the time. The population is still growing. And from a water supply perspective, unfortunately, it's often growing the fastest in places where we can least afford it, from a water supply standpoint. If you look at the numbers on this map to the right, those represent population growth. And the shading represents per capita water usage. So the dark states with big numbers really pose a problem when you consider that we're a country with an aging infrastructure. We haven't invested in it the same way we have in years past. And yet somehow, our population is growing. We hope that our economy will continue to grow. And water is a big part of that. Our public supply water system is really one of the engines to economic growth. And certainly population growth in this country. So we need to figure out a way to continue to meet that need. Unfortunately -- everyone remembers five or six years ago, we had the stimulus, and we had this sudden surge of money that we spent on infrastructure. Well, we took -- we spent about $6 billion back then on water infrastructure projects. Our need over the next 20 years is on the order of $700 billion. So the "surge" in investment that we had and we're not likely to see again was $6 billion. Our needs, about $700 billion. That math doesn't really add up. Additionally, we're expecting water shortages in over 40 states. Now, this report has kind of circulated in the news lately, and a lot of times people miss the point. The take-home point to me of that report is that those are non-drought water shortages. So 40 out of the 50 states, given average supply, average demand, are expecting to not have enough water to meet their needs in the coming years. Now, we're not so lucky as to not have drought. We know we're going to get drought. We're a big enough country. We're varied enough in terms of climate that every year we're going to face drought. So it's just another layer of complexity onto this challenge of continuing to supply clean, healthy, safe drinking water.

Next slide:
So what's that done to the cost of water? Let's make no mistake. Water is still really, really cheap in this country right now. It's about $10 for 1,000 gallons water and wastewater combined, is what people found average across the country. But over the last few years, we started to do something really interesting. This is CPI, so that's the consumer price index. Relative to that line, things are getting more expensive when they're above that line, less expensive when they're below it. And let's look at how it compares to some of the utilities. Housing tends to track pretty closely to CPI because most of us spend an awful lot of money on housing. Electricity, even though you might not want to admit it the day you pay your electric bill, but relative to the price of other goods, electricity has really stayed relatively affordable over the last few years. Natural gas, as many of us are aware, is a little more volatile. In the winter of 2012, a lot of us to heat our homes in the northeast or the northern part of the country, really appreciated that dip you see in natural gas, because it saved us a lot of money. I would like to look at gasoline, too. Because that's really sort of the most consistent price signal we get when it comes to energy, right? By the time I pay my electric bill, I've already used that electricity a month ago. There's no real feedback. But I see the price of gas all the time, and I pay the price of gas all the time. So just to sort of underscore what this difference makes, anyone remember paying for gas in 1998? I think the national average that year was under $2. It was like ... $1.03 in 1998. By the time we get to 2012, the average cost of gasoline in the country was $3.62. So this is what one dollar and three cents to $3.62 looks like in terms of CPI. And now this is water. The only thing in the entire CPI catalog that comes close to matching water and sewer -- I hate to tell it, for people that have teenagers and high school kids -- is college tuition. The really scary part about this, is that most people are -- most people believe that we have not yet seen the major increase in the cost of water and sewer. So, and I like to say, apparently the national rate is about $10 per 1,000 gallons. Average person's use is about 100 gallons a day, all told. So if we add that up, for a family of four the average water bill is somewhere in the low triple digits, high double-digits per month. So right then and there, if there are low-cost, no-cost ways that we can save 20, 30 percent of that, that's very welcome savings. But in a program like Zero Energy Ready Homes, where we're very much thinking about what technologies and what resources do we need to put in these homes, so that they can perform 50 years from now, well, what's water going to cost in 20, 30, 40, 50 years? And at what point does that go from being very welcome savings and it's nice to have, to really being something that we can't afford to not do? In my opinion, I think we're really knocking on the door here. And certainly in 20 years, I think we're not going to be able -- people are not going to be able to afford water-gushing homes.

Next slide:
So it's also important to remember, and Jamie touched on this briefly, is that saving water is not just about saving the gallons of water. We spend a tremendous amount of energy delivering and treating clean water, and treating wastewater on the other end, in addition to heating water inside of our homes. Nationally, we spend about 3 to 4 percent of the entire grid on treating and moving water. In certain parts of the country it's even higher. In California, we spend 20 percent of all energy on simply getting water around to where it needs to be. It's also a major cost for most municipalities. So there's really no doubt about it, that water and energy are really connected in a very inextricable way. And that's why WaterSense is here. We're here because we need water efficiency. We're going to need to meet our infrastructure challenges. We're going to need it to protect our communities from drought. And we're going to need it to protect consumers, who aren't going to be able to afford to consume water at the same rate they're doing it now, when utility prices continue to rise. So the WaterSense label is here to identify products in addition to homes that are third-party certified in both efficiency and performance.

Next slide:
The performance aspect is obviously very important. Most of us are probably old enough to remember the first-generation low-flow toilets that didn't work all that well. Because we're now able to actually put performance criteria into the specification, that's rapidly becoming a thing of the past. So when you look for the WaterSense label, you can know that the product is both very efficient and high- performing. We also aim to promote water efficiency behaviors and actions. Of course, in doing so we help save consumers money. We reduce the need to expand infrastructure in the future, and we're able to save water for critical needs as a nation.

Next slide:
Let's talk a little bit more about what the actual requirements are for WaterSense Labeled Homes. Just a couple of quick relatively administrative notes. Most people -- it sounded like most people weren't too familiar with the WaterSense Labeled program. For those that are and may have seen, we did release a revision this past July. Revision 1.2. And all that does is actually reflect changes that we made in our irrigation partnership program. So no real substantive changes to the requirements. I just want people to know. There's no reason to go circling back to your projects that might be seeking the WaterSense label right now and reviewing all of the criteria. It's really just a change that reflects the updates to the irrigation partners. We touched on on this briefly. Thanks for the question that we got. Homes that are eligible for a WaterSense Labeled Home, are both single-family homes and townhomes, as well as homes in multifamily buildings that are up to three stories in size. Or more than three stories, providing that the units have independent heating, cooling, and hot water systems, separate from other units. That's not to say that you can't have a very efficient building using a central boiler. Or other central systems. But our criteria really isn't set up to capture the water use associated with those, so that's all that we can apply the WaterSense label to. And it does apply to both new construction and existing homes, but, as I mentioned, the criteria remains the same. So it is a little bit of a heavier lift for existing homes. How we third-party certify homes. The program has an administrator, which is RESNET, just as the HERS system does, for those -- many of you are familiar with it. There is a provider role, just as there is in -- there is accredited rating providers. There are approved WaterSense providers. And finally, there's the water efficiency inspector who might be independent, might work directly with the rater, who actually physically comes and does the inspection, and most of the time that's actually a HERS rater. And I would say for those of you who are interested in pursuing the label, the thing to do is to work through your existing relationships. If you are builder, I'd talk to your existing raters and find out if it's something that they offer or something they're planning to offer in the future. If you're a rater, then certainly talk to your provider and find out if they're also a WaterSense label provider, since there's a good chance that they are, and they can provide the training and do the quality assurance so you're able to do inspections and offer your clients the label. If you are a provider and you're interested, feel free to contact me. You can also contact RESNET directly but we'd be happy to walk you through the process.

Next slide:
So what do we require in a WaterSense Labeled Home? Well, of course, we require WaterSense labeled products. Like I said, those are one of those really easy ways to add both performance and efficiency to a home. They're third-party certified for both efficiency and performance, so you know they're going to save water. In the case of showers and faucets, you know they're going to save energy by using less water.

Next slide:
You know that they're going to perform well. They're available for -- you can find the label on showerheads, faucets, and toilets. Products are available at literally every price point. And like I said, it's just a very easy, most of the time no-cost, way to add both efficiency and performance, resulting in a better performing home. We also have requirements for leak prevention and service pressure. I'll talk a little bit about those. Again, one of those instances where a small change in behavior and a small change in practices on the construction side can really lead to a tremendous amount of value down the line, in terms of making sure that you're delivering a home that's free of leaks and is going to continue to perform well. We'll talk a little bit more in-depth about hot water distribution in a second, but as I mentioned before, that is one of the required elements of Zero Energy Ready Homes. Of course, all homes to bear the label are third-party certified, and they all feature smart landscaping and efficient, if it's included, irrigation.

Next slide:
So does anyone know what this little device is, over here? Name something like it? It's a pressure gauge. Costs about $10 at any plumbing supply store, and we use this to do a pressure loss test in the inspection process. So, it's really simple to do. Hook it up to a hose bib. You might have to go into the laundry hookup, if there's a separate irrigation outside on a separate loop. But hook it up to the hose bib. Shut up the water at the main shutoff. And go back and look at the pressure gauge. The reason we're doing this, if you think about it, the plumbing system is a closed system. If the pressure is changing when the water is shut off to the rest of the public supply system, it's got to be getting out someplace. So if the pressure is fluctuating, then somebody's using water. Water is getting out to the system somehow. And if no one is actively using water, that means that you have a leak. I'd love to tell you that this test never comes back positive on new homes. That's just not true. We find leaks in new homes all the time. It's not always what we expect. When we talk about leaks, everyone tends to think about 10,000 gallons in the basement. But the reality is, more times than not, the leak is a toilet flapper that's installed a little bit askew. Or it's a faucet that drips just enough -- not so much that you catch it in a quick walk-through, but enough so that the sum total of that, of the water that's escaping from it, is substantial when it's dripping 24 hours a day, seven days a week. So again, only takes a few minutes, doesn't cost anything, and yet it's a great way to check and make sure that the home you're delivering actually doesn't leak.

Next slide:
Another thing we check for when do that is service pressure. Now, this makes people nervous because people often complain about pressure in the shower. That's why we don't like low-flow showerheads, because they don't have enough pressure. What people characterize as pressure in that sense is really force. We're talking about with what level of force the water hits their body. Typically code will let you go anywhere from 45 to 85 psi. And all we've done with WaterSense is we've restricted a little bit, down to 60 psi. The reason that we've done that, is that A, there's a water savings there. The products aren't going to flow above the maximum threshold when we do that. B, it reduces the likelihood of leaks. Again, and is a little bit safer for the home. But also, WaterSense labeled products are tested at their performance level all the way down to 45 psi. The 60 psi, or the 45 to 60 psi, is more than enough to make sure that they're still performing with adequate force. The other nice thing this does is provides a little bit of insurance against fluctuations that might be happening in the public supply system. So, a lot of times when we're working on new home construction, we're dealing with areas that are expanding. And multiple homes are being brought online and being hooked up to the system, and they're experiencing changes in terms of the pressure at the point of delivery. So installing a -- excuse me -- a PRV is another way to make sure that the experience that the homeowner or the occupant has inside the home doesn't change over time and doesn't result in callbacks. There's another thing we do in WaterSense Labeled Homes that I really love. And it's homeowner / occupant education. We have a great template we provide to our builder partners that makes it very easy to pass on all the information about the technology that the homeowner's going to need to know to maintain all the technology, all the equipment, and all of the great water-saving features of that home. As a homeowner, personally the reason I love this is, I spent the last two years trying to recreate this in my home. And we've given builders a great way to do it in a really simple, easy manner, which again, just is a tremendous added value to the homeowner.

Next slide:
OK, I'm going to jump into hot water distribution. And again, this is part of the WaterSense requirements that have been drafted and are required in the WaterSense Labeled Homes. Excuse me, is required in Zero Energy Ready Homes. So you heard Jamie provide a little bit of background in it. I think we've all experienced this. This is when Mr. and Mrs. Jones wakes up in the morning. They go take the shower. They turn on their hot water, and they wait. And they're waiting because the water's not actually hot. It used to be hot. It's coming from the hot water system. But what's happened is, it's been pumped out into the walls and allowed to cool down, and we need to wait for fresh hot water to get there. Huge waste of energy, huge waste of water. Poor-performing solution. So the requirement that we have in WaterSense Labeled Homes is that we're not going to allow any more than a half-gallon of water between the furthest fixture and the source of hot water. And when we do a test, we're actually going to test it at 0.6. We've going to give it 20 percent, because you're going to lose some water, you're going to lose some heat as the water moves through the system, and there's going to be just a little bit of water stored in the actual product.

Next slide:
So we do test that at a different level than the requirement actually says. We do allow recirculation systems, but we do require that they're demand initiated. So they need to be push-button, or motion-sensitive or a motion sensor. We also allow sort of this new breed of schedule adaptive water heaters. So these are water heaters which almost -- I liken them to a nest thermostat. They learn the tendencies and practices of the home, and they're able to recirculate based on that information. We don't allow products that are simply timer or temperature based recirculation systems. So products that have temperature gauges and say, OK, well, water's at 85 degrees, I'm going to spin the water around the loop. Or products that simply say, OK, 7 a.m., I'm going to recirculate the water. The reason we don't allow those is, No. 1, there's really conflicting information about how much water those actually save. Because it's so varied, based on the individual practices of the home. The other is that we do know that they have the potential to use a lot of energy. So even though WaterSense obviously is a water efficiency program, we certainly don't want to be using energy in the name of water efficiency. And so we do prohibit those types of technologies. So once again, I'm going to take a step back, and I'm going to ask why do we even need this requirement? Why did we get to the point where the solutions that we're putting in most homes are so poor performing?
