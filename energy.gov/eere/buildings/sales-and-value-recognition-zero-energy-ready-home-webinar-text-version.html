So these homes are homes of the future that are available today. So where we have building scientists in construction, you have to remember that we have inventory scientists in real estate. And you can say, at that point in time, if you're asking the appraiser questions and you're not getting a comfort level, that's your cue to go back to the lender and say, hey, this is a different product. The appraiser that wants to come out, I'm not comfortable that they're qualified to do this. Can we go back and change things? And that's where you want to use appraisal addendum and say, look, this is someone that's got to have experience with these kind of factors on the home. And so that's my free advice for today, is the more you can do to pre-screen, your life becomes much easier to get and pay for the right appraiser the first time. Because the stuff that Sandy's talking about can be kind of complicated and a big headache.

Sandy Adomatis:
And I agree with Laura. You have a right to ask those questions up front. And an appraiser should answer your questions to give you comfort level there.

Joe Nebbia:
OK, we have time for maybe one more question. And the question is, can you talk a little bit, either of you, about why a HERS Index as opposed to something like say utility bills would be used for the green MLS or for appraisals in this process?

Sandy Adomatis:
Well, I can give you a great example why sometimes utility bills don't really mirror the energy efficiency of the structure. Locally, I was appraising an energy-efficient home. It was a green rated home, from plans and specs. And I was searching MLS and came up with a house that said energy efficient and in the comments section it said, the highest utility bill ever has been $125 a month. I got all excited and called the Realtor and found out that the Realtor said, well, that's really not a good comp. I said, why isn't it a good comp? He said, well, no one ever lived in the home. Now with the HERS Index, we know that the occupant level is one per bedroom, plus one. So, the appraisers are gaining more knowledge about the HERS Index. Now, if we use utility bills -- I'm not saying we can't use them -- but if the appraiser uses them, then on our subject property and on our comparables, we need to have some knowledge of how many occupants, is there any unusual plug load, is it a year-round home, or is it seasonal only? So there's a lot of things that come into play, and that it may be just that the occupants are energy-efficient and not the structure. So they may be turning the temperature way down or whatever. I do a study in the book that I wrote for my actual subdivision, where I used actual utility bills. And so if you get a chance to read that, you'll get a better feel for what I'm describing here.

Laura Reedy Stukel:
And let me just add, I hear a lot, I think the building community, remodeling, construction, whatever it is, it feels like I hear a lot that people want to get to one thing. And the experience that I've had is much different on the real estate side. And so we want a list of features, which most often can be -- a high level of that can be provided in the verification program. So some kind of high-level idea that this is different from a code home. We want the performance metrics like a HERS score or a home energy score, that's helpful and that tells us that the features that are there are contributing to performance. And then what I call the "BS Test" is that we like the utility bills because it provides the context for, is the performance metric accurate? So I think a lot of times people want just one thing, but consumers and real estate agents and appraisers, it's all so new to us, so we really are in a stage where we need those. Kind of it's a three- legged stool. So what are the features or measures, what's the performance, and then how is that established in the real world utility bills? So at least for the short term -- the next five, 10 years -- really if you can cover all three of those items, you give a lot more confidence to the real estate market. And then one other anecdote that I'll share, is that when we implemented that utility sharing information in Chicago, we found that that was very well- received, because cost, that's what consumers think about. And we joke that people factor in how many boxes of mac and cheese they might have to buy to go up to a certain home, or you know, sometimes the mac and cheese conversation comes in. But that's what they know. They know tax, they know mortgage, and utility cost is something that resonates a lot to them, at that point in time. And a lot of times consumers ask to see the utility bills. So we do see that in Chicago. I know of a couple MLSes that are talking about that. I've had some good conversations with the folks at Green Button. So there's certainly some opportunities that are possible in the future where we do make the utility costs at least more available to consumers, and then in the context of some of this other rich information that you're providing to us, by participating in programs like Zero Energy Ready Home, which gives us that context about features.
