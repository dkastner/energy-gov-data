Next slide:

So from a very basic building that we used to build, shown in this picture, we now have much more insulation, we have much better windows, and much, much more airtight construction. So let's build off this and look at three key risks that we face because of this new reality.

Next slide:

And of course, moisture is the No. 1 concern of homeowners, by far the most calls that the research center gets at the Housing Innovation Research Lab. And what happens when we have an advanced enclosure is the inside surface and the cavity on the cold side -- in cold climates that would be the outside sheathing; in hot climates that would be the sheetrock facing the cavity -- it's getting much colder. And that's because there's less thermal flow going through. And because there's less thermal flow, there's less drying. The wall cannot dry if it gets wet. So the risk that we're facing is more wetting potential while homes will have less drying that they can withstand, less tolerance for drying.

Next slide:
So we have to address this risk, and we do it with a comprehensive water protection system. So we have water-managed roofs, we want to have water-managed walls and openings, water- managed site and foundation details, and inside the home where water is present we want materials that are chosen carefully. Basically these are tried-and-true, proven measures. The key is not to just leave out one or two; that can get you into trouble. But to have a complete system of water protection.

Next slide:
Risk No. 2 has to deal again with the beginning of this advanced enclosure as a new reality, and it addresses whether we provide comfort with these advanced homes. And the outcome of an advanced enclosure is we have much, much reduced heating and cooling loads. And as a result, our heating and cooling systems have much less air flow going through the ducts. At the same time, we are starting the cooling much later in the year and the heating much later in the year, so we have longer swing seasons. And during those longer swing seasons, we have less humidity control. So we have to worry about relative humidity. And with the less air flow, we are running the risk of providing inadequate mixing, because up to now we've had very limited and often no attention paid to duct sizing, and to terminal design and whether there is adequate flow or adequate throw of the air into the room from the registers and how the registers are selected. So we need to address that risk with an optimized comfort system.

Next slide:
And the first thing we have to do is understand the loads and then right-size the equipment and/or use variable speed equipment that will enable the heating and cooling to meet the load requirements. Then we need the ducts inside the conditioned space. We can no longer tolerate running cooling through an oven, a hot, 150-degree attic anymore. Or running heated air through a freezing condition in the attic or crawl space or whatever it may be. And the other thing we need is proper installation. We need to know basic metrics are being followed that are specified by the manufacturer, and we need to test them and verify that the systems are installed correctly. And this is based on a long history of systems going in without that scrutiny.

Next slide:
And so the solution is that kind of optimized comfort system. The last risk is ensured indoor air quality. Again, starting with the advanced enclosure, we have much less air exchange with the tight construction, and contaminants can build up. So therefore, we have that risk that we have to manage. And the way we manage it is with a complete indoor air quality system, not a partial system. We don't leave out key pieces that can get us in trouble. So we start first with source control. We keep things out that can get us into trouble, sometimes radon, sometimes biological contaminants. And then we also build with materials that don't have lots of dangerous contaminants already built into them. And we also select equipment that doesn't introduce combustion byproducts. So we basically are managing the sources very carefully. The second part of a complete system is a fresh air system. Many of you will know of this as a ventilation system. And essentially it dilutes the residual contaminants that still wind up in our homes. And lastly, high-capture filtration in the form of a high-MERV filter in our heating and cooling system that can much more effectively capture particulates that are very, very high impact on health and homes. And so that's our complete indoor air quality system.

Next slide:

Let's say for example you have the 2009 IECC code; we know the 2012 is coming around the corner. So what we should do to give the consumer the best value is to optimize the enclosure. Give them the 2020 model car today in 2016. And the way we do that -- in doing that, what we wind up doing is having less obsolescence, because that home will not be obsolete in the near future, three or four years when the code jumps up, to in this case an example of 2012 IECC. And as energy costs continue to inflate, we have more future protection from those high costs. And very simply, we're going to ratchet up the insulation, the windows, and the air sealing to meet future code requirements. Have a whole-house thermal protection system that's ready for the future.

Next slide:

We do the comprehensive water protection system we mentioned to manage risk, and we wind up with a house that delivers peace of mind. Again, I mention that the No. 1 consumer concern about durability with home ownership is with managing moisture problems. And with a house that has a complete water protection system, you are directly providing differentiation, addressing that concern.

Next slide:
The next differentiation opportunity is providing ultimate comfort, comfort that consumers really haven't yet seen, across the large realm of home ownership in this country. And what's happening is we've optimized our comfort system for low loads, and now we have something truly special. First, with the enclosure we're now doing something homes have hardly done up to now. We're managing the surface temperatures or the mean radiant temperature in our homes. When you walk in a home that has the surface temperatures under control, it's a completely different comfort experience. And also with the HVAC systems that are sized properly and managed humidity, we have air flow and RH under control. So this is a completely different comfort experience, and it protects the builder, as well, because homeowner complaints are much, much less likely when those factors are under control.

Next slide:
The fourth opportunity for differentiation, it gets back to the indoor air quality issue. We've come back with a comprehensive indoor air quality system. So these homes are truly health- ready. Now the homeowners themselves have lots of behavior that can affect the ultimate health performance of the home, but the builder has delivered a package that truly positions these homes to live in a much more healthful lifestyle for the homeowners. We will often hear stories in our experience talking to homeowners and builders about how in some cases the homeowner is able to throw away inhalers for their children because, again, the house lives much more healthy.

Next slide:
The last opportunity to talk about for differentiation or second to last is about technology, and the interest today in advanced technology in all facets of our lives. With the optimized enclosure and the ultra-low loads, now over half the remaining energy use in most homes will be attributed to all the things that go inside. So now we have energy-efficient components throughout the house, and the house is truly delivering better- quality products throughout the home, products that will often perform better and have better control capabilities. And so this is truly a tech-ready home for each and every home buyer.

Next slide:

All we need are low-cost or no-cost details that can make our homes ready for solar. And so there's minimal disruption or cost penalty in the future anytime buyers would want to install a solar system. And so your homes are zero ready, and this is particularly the case in the Zero Energy Ready Home program, for homes located in regions where there's a meaningful solar resource.

And then the solar-ready construction so the house is zero-ready. This is in fact a home to the power of zero, and consumers can bank on this performance because it's a trusted label from the Department of Energy that verifies a home meets this level of excellence.
