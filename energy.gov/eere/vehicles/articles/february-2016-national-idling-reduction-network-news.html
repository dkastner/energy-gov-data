February 2016 National Idling Reduction Network News: From the Editor

A Long Story, Short, about APU Weight Exemptions

There’s a long story, which goes back many years, in this map. We use it to show how states have recognized the need to reduce emissions while also accommodating long-haul truckers who need to be comfortable in their sleeper cabs during their federally mandated rest periods. But it wasn’t always this way.

The story goes back to 2005 when the Energy Policy Act of 2005 (EPAct 2005) was signed into law. Until that time, the weight of an auxiliary power unit (APU) that kept the trucker warm in the winter and cool in the summer without idling counted toward the total weight of the tractor and its trailer.

Some trucks carry light loads, like potato chips, and other carry heavy loads, like lead-acid batteries, but they all are supposed to weigh no more than 80,000 pounds when they travel on the Federal Interstate Highway System. An APU can weigh a lot, perhaps 400 pounds or more. In trucks carrying denser loads, such as batteries, APUs are more likely to bump the truck above the maximum allowable weight, resulting in possible penalties.

Section 756 of EPAct 2005 allowed states to grant truckers a 400-pound weight exemption for APUs. Many, but not all, states did so. Some states allow an exemption by law and others simply by enforcement policy.

As technology progressed, so did legislation. In 2012, Congress passed Moving Ahead for Progress in the 21st Century (MAP-21). Section 1510 of MAP-21 upped the weight exemption to 550 pounds to accommodate the heavier, newly emerging battery-powered APUs.

When we found out recently that Arkansas allows a 550-pound weight exemption by enforcement policy, we began to wonder if other states have changed their policies without our being aware of these changes. So, to make this long story short, we are asking our readers to help us update this map.

If APU weight exemptions are on your radar, please look over the map and let us know if anything seems to be out of date. We want to provide the most accurate information possible for everyone in the National Idling Reduction Network.

Thanks for your help.

 

Terry M. Levinson, Editor
Allegheny Science & Technology
tlevinson@alleghenyst.com

 

Patricia Weikersheimer, Writer
Argonne National Laboratory
pweikersheimer@anl.gov

Regulatory News

DERA Funding Update

The White House’s proposed budget for fiscal year (FY) 2017 requests $10 million for EPA’s Diesel Emissions Reduction Act (DERA) grant program, down from the FY 2016 $50 million final appropriation. However, the President’s proposed 21st Century Clean Transportation System, a major initiative to increase investments in clean transportation by about 50%, would provide up to $300 million for DERA in FY 2017. This initiative would be funded by a proposed $10/barrel fee on oil to be paid by oil companies and phased in over 5 years.

In the meantime, the U.S. Senate has approved funding for DERA through an amendment to Senate Bill (SB) 2012, the Energy Policy Modernization Act of 2015. The amendment calls for an allocation of $100 million for 5 years (FY 2017 through FY 2021).

More information about the President’s proposed budget is available at the White House's press office website, as is a fact sheet on the President's 21st Century Clean Transportation System.

School Transportation News offers more information about the Senate’s amendment to SB 2012.

Manufacturers' News

Battery-Powered Cooling Now Factory-Installed Option on Volvo Trucks

Volvo Trucks North America has introduced battery-powered, factory-installed cooling for a series of its sleeper trucks. The system, which the manufacturer calls a “parking cooler,” is powered by four absorbed glass mat (AGM) deep-cycle batteries in combination with the truck’s starting batteries. According to a company press release, the cooler is rated to keep the sleeper at a temperature of 74°F for up to 10 hours. It is best suited to trucks that are driven at least 6 hours before being stopped for the driver’s required rest period.

For trucks equipped with the optional parking heater, one thermostat will control both air conditioning and heat. More information is available at the Volvo Trucks website.

 

Ports

Sea-Tac’s Success with eGSE

One way airports reduce fuel use and emissions is through the electrification of ground-support equipment (GSE), such as baggage tugs, belt loaders, and aircraft-pushback vehicles. eGSE, as it’s called, not only saves fuel and produces no tailpipe emissions, but is quiet compared to conventionally fueled GSE.

At Seattle Tacoma International Airport (Sea-Tac), where eGSE infrastructure and equipment has been funded in part by the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act (ARRA), Alaska Air Group has cut fuel consumption by 250,000 gallons annually, for a savings of about $500,000/year. The payback period on the incremental cost of the new equipment is about 1 year. Sea-Tac has begun working with other tenant airlines to acquire more eGSE.

For more information, please see the U.S. DOE’s Alternative Fuels Data Center website.

 

Other News of Interest

Argonne Study: Emissions Related to Idling Reduction Technologies

Investigators at Argonne National Laboratory have long researched the emissions associated with the extended idling of Class 8 trucks. In light of the increasingly stringent emissions standards for these trucks—without corresponding changes for small engines, such as those that power APUs—they decided to take another look. In their study, “Emissions from Idling Heavy-Duty Trucks and Idling-Reduction Equipment,” they conclude that while idling reduction technologies always reduce fuel use and carbon dioxide (CO2) emissions, they may not reduce the emission of pollutants such as particulate matter (PM) on trucks equipped with diesel particulate filters (DPFs). The paper is available on the Argonne website.
