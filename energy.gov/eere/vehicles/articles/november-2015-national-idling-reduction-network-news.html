November 2015 National Idling Reduction Network News: From the Editor

Is Start-Stop in Your Future?

Drivers of hybrid and electric vehicles are accustomed to a feature called “start-stop” that saves fuel by turning the vehicle off when you’re stopped (e.g., waiting in traffic) and restarting it when you’re ready to move. However, if you want a new, conventional automobile or light-duty truck, you might consider buying one that uses the same technology. You can now find it in a range of American automobiles, including Cadillac CT6s, Chevy Malibus, Ford Fusions, and Ford F-150 trucks.

Start-stop (also known as auto start, idle stop, auto start-stop, start/stop, or mild hybrid) technology senses when your foot is on the brake for a predetermined period of time and shuts off the engine. All your auxiliaries remain on while start-stop is engaged. When you release the brake, the engine seamlessly starts up again. The technology underlying start-stop includes a heavy-duty starter that can endure 300,000 cycles instead of 30,000 cycles found in most vehicles.

Europeans have long used this technology, as their fuel prices are as much as three times higher than ours. In addition, start-stop engineering is less complicated for manual-transmission vehicles, which are more common in Europe than here in the States. Finally, current U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) fuel-economy test procedures don’t account for much simulated idling, so start-stop–associated savings may not even show up on the label of the new car, although this may change in the near future.

I recently took a few test drives of both American- and German-made automobiles equipped with start-stop. Each of the vehicles had an indicator similar to the one below to show when the feature was in use.


Indicators of start-stop technology in a 2015 Chevrolet Malibu. The left photo shows the indicator when the vehicle is stationary but not idling; the right photo shows the indicator when the vehicle is stationary and idling. Used with permission of Intertek Testing Services NA.

At first, the silence and lack of vibration at traffic lights were quite noticeable, but it didn’t take long to adjust to the quiet. Then, in the instant that it took for me to move my foot from the brake to the accelerator, the engine came back to life. I was expecting this experience, but if I hadn’t known about it, I would have been quite disconcerted. Automobile manufacturers do allow the start-stop feature to be turned completely turned off.

So how much fuel can you save by having this technology in your car or light truck? To quote EPA, “Your mileage may vary,” and savings depend on traffic and driving style. Fuel savings probably range from about 3% to more than 10% for stop-and-go city traffic. However, your driving habits strongly influence your actual fuel economy.

Please let us know about your experience with this technology.

 

Terry M. Levinson, Editor
Allegheny Science & Technology
tlevinson@alleghenyst.com

 

Patricia Weikersheimer, Writer

Regulatory

Houston Passes Idling Ordinance

The Houston City Council recently passed, and the mayor approved, an idling ordinance. Now, trucks weighing more than 14,000 lb are prohibited from idling for more than 5 minutes. According to an article published in the Houston Chronicle, on November 4 (“Houston Council Approves Anti-Idling Law”), emergency and military vehicles are exempt from the ordinance, as are vehicles being loaded or unloaded. Also exempt are vehicles idling for safety and health reasons, including defrosting windshields and running heat or air conditioning. Transit vehicles carrying passengers may idle for up to 15 minutes if needed for heat or air conditioning.

For more information, please see the Houston Chronicle.

 

U.S. House Bill Supports Clean Vehicle Technologies

A bill recently introduced in the U.S. House seeks to promote investments in research and development for clean vehicle technologies. House of Representatives (H.R.) Bill 4106, also called the Vehicle Innovation Act (VIA), names mild hybrid (stop-start) technology, power-pack modeling and testing, and electric vehicle charging technology as among the many promising technologies.

VIA, which was introduced by State Representative Debbie Dingell (D-MI), has been referred to committee. For more information, please visit truckinginfo.com. To follow the bill, please go to congress.gov.

Electrified Parking Spaces

IdleAir Now Available in Hubbard, Ohio

IdleAir has opened a new location, with 30 parking spaces, at the Flying J Travel Plaza in Hubbard, Ohio. In 2013, Shorepower Technologies equipped this Flying J with 24 electrified parking spaces, including connections for refrigerated trailers. The Hubbard Flying J is thus the first site to offer both Shorepower and IdleAir services to long-haul truckers.

IdleAir is offering a special through March 31, 2016, at its three Ohio locations: $1/hr for the ConvoyTV+Power package, which includes access to 120-V power, DIRECTV, and basic Internet. For more information, please see IdleAir’s website.

 

Ports

Southern California Ports Surpass Air Quality Improvement Goals

Through a variety of initiatives, including the Clean Trucks Program and the use of shore power for cargo ships, the Ports of Los Angeles (POLA) and Long Beach (POLB) have exceeded their air-pollution reduction goals for 2014, as described in recently released reports.

The 2006 San Pedro Bay Ports Clean Air Action Plan, a joint enterprise of POLA and POLB with input from the South Coast Air Quality Management District, CARB, and the U.S. EPA, called for a significant reduction in port-generated pollutants from all vehicle and equipment types. Specifically, by 2014, the ports were to reduce diesel particulate matter (DPM) emissions by 72%, nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions by 22%, and sulfur oxide (SOx) emissions by 93% over 2005 levels.

In 2014, POLA’s DPM levels were down 85% from 2005 levels, and SOx and NOx levels were down 97% and 52%, respectively. POLB in 2014 achieved an 85% reduction in DPM over 2005 levels. The NOx and SOx levels were reduced by 50% and 97%, respectively.

The San Pedro Bay Ports Clean Air Action Plan is an ongoing initiative. The websites of POLA and POLB provide more information.

 

Other News of Interest

PG&E: Hybrid-Electric Bucket Trucks Reduce Idling to "Basically Zero"

In 2014, San Francisco–based energy provider Pacific Gas and Electric Company (PG&E) launched its Class 5 plug-in hybrid-electric bucket truck. With up to 40 miles of all-electric range, the truck provides battery-electric power for not only hydraulics, but heat, air conditioning, lighting, and tools needed at job sites. According to Dave Meisel, PG&E’s Senior Director of Transportation and Aviation Services, the trucks have reduced idling “from four to six hours a day to basically zero.”

The hybrid-electric bucket trucks were developed in partnership with Efficient Drivetrains Incorporated. In addition, PG&E collaborated with the Department of Energy’s National Renewable Energy Laboratory to evaluate its performance. For more information, please go to truckinginfo.com or the DOE’s Office of Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy’s website.
