The National Idling Reduction Network brings together trucking and transit companies; railroads; ports; equipment manufacturers; Federal, state, and local government agencies (including regulators); nonprofit organizations; and national research laboratories to identify consistent, workable solutions to heavy-vehicle idling for the entire United States. October 2015 National Idling Reduction Network News: From the Editor

Work Truck Fleets Recognize the Need for Idling Reduction

There are many types of work trucks: utility trucks and their buckets that fix utility lines, refuse haulers that pick up our trash, delivery trucks that carry consumer goods, and many other vocational vehicles. We see them every day, yet in some ways they’re invisible unless they block our streets or make a lot of noise idling.

To provide more insight into this group of more than eight million vehicles, NTEA, a trade organization for more than 1,750 member organizations, released a report this summer on work truck idling vis-à-vis operating efficiency. The report, “2015 Work Truck Electrification and Idle Management Study – Exploring Advanced Vehicle Technologies,” is filled with graphs and charts on the findings of its in-depth member survey in early 2015.

The NTEA survey found that one-third of the respondent fleets idle for 1-2 hours per day and another third idle less than 1 hour. A smaller fraction, 8% of the fleets, idled more than 4 hours. These numbers make sense given that one-quarter of the fleets are from the utility/telecom industry where idling is commonplace.


Reproduced with permission from NTEA.

According to the report, the top three solutions to these issues are: “(1) training drivers to idle less, (2) reducing incidental idling, and (3) timed engine shutdown.” Grants or tax incentives also played a role in persuading fleets to pursue these enhancements. Key for fleets was not only reducing operational costs, but acceptable payback period for equipment installed.

NTEA’s report also notes the growing importance of telematics. Understanding where your vehicles are (or should be) and how long they’re there (or not) and doing what they’re supposed to be doing (or not) can strongly influence what decisions a fleet manager makes regarding idling reduction.

Please let us know of your experiences in this regard.

 

Terry M. Levinson, Editor
Allegheny Science & Technology
tlevinson@alleghenyst.com

 

Patricia Weikersheimer, Writer

Regulatory

North Carolina Repeals Idling Law

North Carolina Governor Pat McCrory has signed into law a bill that repeals the state’s heavy-duty vehicle idling restriction passed in 2010. Although House Bill 765, “Regulatory Reform Act of 2015,” calls for the Secretary of Environment and Natural Resources to repeal the idling law no later than March 1, 2016, it also states that the law shall not be implemented or enforced before then, effectively making the repeal immediate.

The repealed law prohibited the idling of 10,000+ lb heavy-duty vehicles for more than 5 minutes in a 60-minute period. The text of North Carolina’s Regulatory Reform Act of 2015 as signed by Governor McCrory is available at the North Carolina legislature website.

 

Houston To Vote on Idling Restriction

On November 4, the Houston City Council is scheduled to vote on whether or not to adopt the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality’s (TCEQ) idling limitations rule. By Texas law, jurisdictional idling reduction programs must adopt the TCEQ’s idling rule and develop a Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) with the TCEQ for the purpose of enforcement.

The ordinance would build on the voluntary idling reduction policy for public and private fleets Houston adopted in May 2012. To see the proposed ordinance, please go to the Houston City Council’s website.

Education, Outreach, and Campaigns

Vermont Schools

Idle-Free VT, a Vermont-based nonprofit organization that raises awareness about unnecessary vehicle idling, has received $18,500 from the High Meadows Fund and the Vermont Community Foundation to implement the Vermont Idle-Free Schools project. Through the initiative, Idle-Free VT will work with school administrators and teachers, as well as others in the community, to conduct 60 idling awareness and eco-driving educational sessions for more than 1,000 middle- and high-school students. For more information, please see IdleFree VT’s Idle-Free Schools web page.
