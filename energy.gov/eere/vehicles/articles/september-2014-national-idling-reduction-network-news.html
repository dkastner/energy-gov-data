The National Idling Reduction Network brings together trucking and transit companies; railroads; ports; equipment manufacturers; Federal, state, and local government agencies (including regulators); nonprofit organizations; and national research laboratories to identify consistent, workable solutions to heavy-vehicle idling for the entire United States. Below is the September 2014 issue of the National Idling Reduction Network News; the archives are available on the Archives page and the latest issue is on the main NIRNN page.

September 2014 National Idling Reduction Network News: From the Editor

As we continue our walk down memory lane celebrating 10 years of publication, we'd like to look at how weight exemptions for auxiliary power units (APUs) have changed since 2005.

On the Federal Interstate Highway System, long-haul trucks are generally limited to a gross vehicle weight (GVW) of 80,000 lb. That limit includes the weight of the truck and trailer as well as the truck’s cargo and fuel. Obviously, drivers hauling boxes of potato chips have less of a problem with weight limits than haulers of lead-acid batteries.

When the trucking industry began to realize the value of using APUs to save fuel during rest periods, drivers hauling heavy cargo were at a disadvantage. Although APUs offer considerable benefits, they are heavy and that weight counts towards the 80,000-lb limit.

Fortunately, in 2005, Congress passed the Energy Policy Act, which revised these standards, along with a wide range of energy issues. To promote the use of idle reduction technologies that reduce fuel use and emissions, Section 756 of EPAct allowed states to permit a 400-lb exemption for vehicles with APUs without risk of federal funding sanctions.

The 400-lb ceiling covered most APUs available in 2005. However, battery-based APUs soon came to the market, many of which weigh more than 400 lb. In 2012, Congress enacted Moving Ahead for Progress in the 21st Century (MAP-21), which in Sec. 1510 further increased the APU weight allowance to 550 lb.

The map below shows the current status for the APU weight exemption across the country. (An interactive version is available on the Vehicle Technologies Office’s idle reduction webpage.) As you can see, a number of states have adopted the 550-lb exemption, but most have not. To complicate matters, there are states that permit a weight exemption not by law but instead by enforcement policy. A few states, including California, permit no APU weight exemption. Time will tell, of course, and we'll provide that information to you as soon as we learn about it.

As always, we welcome your news and comments.

 

Terry M. Levinson, Editor
Energetics Incorporated
tlevinson@energetics.com

Regulatory News

Massachusetts Adopts 400-lb APU Exemption

For years, the Commonwealth of Massachusetts allowed a 400-lb weight allowance for trucks equipped with APUs by enforcement policy, not state law. But with the passage of a new law, the Commonwealth now permits a 400-lb weight exemption by state law. The change, which went into effect on July 1, is recorded at Massachusetts' legislative website. (Source: Heather L. Ness, J.J. Keller & Associates, Inc.)

Manufacturers' News

Smeal: Idle Reduction for Fire Apparatus

Smeal Fire Apparatus (Snyder, Nebraska) has delivered four vehicles equipped with its SG-09 Green Power technology to the City of Markham, Ontario. The SG-09 is a diesel-powered APU that provides electrical and HVAC capabilities for fire apparatus in engine-off mode.

In vehicles equipped with the SG-09 system, after the parking brake is set (and after a preset idle period), the vehicle's main engine shuts off, and the APU is activated. The system monitors water pump and power take-off (PTO) status to ensure uninterrupted function. If additional AC or DC power is needed at a scene, the system can be started manually to provide it. According to Smeal's Vice President of Sales, Jeff Wegner, SG-09 Green Power technology reduces not only fuel use and carbon emissions, but maintenance on the main chassis engine.

More information is available at Smeal's website.

 

Chrysler Offers Stop-Start on Some 2015 Models

Chrysler is offering engine stop-start (ESS) technology as standard equipment on some models of its 2015 Jeep Cherokee and 2015 Chrysler 200. By shutting down the engine at stops, stop-start technology enables fuel savings and reduced emissions. According to the auto manufacturer, when the driver releases the brake pedal, the ESS system automatically restarts the engine within 0.3 second. Chrysler states that ESS technology will provide up to a 3% improvement in fuel economy with a corresponding reduction in CO2 emissions. Chrysler's website contains more information.

 

Other News of Interest

Idling Reduction System for 90 Police SUVs in Columbus, Ohio

After conducting a pilot test of three anti-idling technologies, the city of Columbus, Ohio, is installing the GRIP Idle Management System with Espar heaters on 90 of its police SUVs. According to the city's Fleet Administrator, Kelly Reagan, this may be the first use of anti-idling technology in the U.S. that provides both heat and air conditioning to a fleet of public-safety vehicles.

The auxiliary-battery equipped idle management system is programmed to shut off a vehicle's engine—without interrupting the operation of electrically based accessories—after it's been placed in park and idled for 2 minutes. When outside temperatures are cool, the Espar heater provides heat to the interior of the vehicle. In warmer months, the GRIP system's computer activates the OEM air conditioning to maintain a constant temperature inside the vehicle.

The cost of the devices and installation is about $278,000. According to Government Fleet, Mr. Reagan believes that the equipment will pay for itself in fuel savings within one year. In an interview with National Idling Reduction Network News staff, Mr. Reagan said that a powerful advantage of this idling reduction package is that it requires no vehicle-operator input; everything is automated.

The city will consider additional anti-idling units as savings are demonstrated in the first 90 units. It also plans to purchase 90 to 100 cruisers per year and hopes to fully outfit all 340 front-line cruisers with the GRIP technology within the next few years. More information is available at Government Fleet.

 

ATRI and NACFE Launch Sustainability and Efficiency Resources

The American Transportation Research Institute (ATRI) has introduced a resource on sustainable freight practices. Organized into three sections—Driving Practices, Vehicle Practices, and Public Sector Practices—the resource covers a range of undertakings, including idle reduction, and their costs and benefits. Please see ATRI for more information.

To "support better, more-informed decisions around efficiency solutions," the North American Council for Freight Efficiency (NACFE) and the Carbon War Room (CWR) have launched a new information platform. More information can be found at Trucking Efficiency.
