Vehicle Technologies Office: National Idling Reduction Network News

The National Idling Reduction Network brings together trucking and transit companies; railroads; ports; equipment manufacturers; Federal, state, and local government agencies (including regulators); nonprofit organizations; and national research laboratories to identify consistent, workable solutions to vehicle idling for the entire United States. Below is the most recent issue; the archives are available on the Archives page.

December 2016 National Idling Reduction Network News: From the Editor

New Year's Resolutions

Have you made your New Year's resolution yet? You haven't? Well, I'm still working on mine too, but I have some thoughts that I can share.

In 2017, I Resolve to

  • Work with the PTA at my local school if school buses are idling unnecessarily. Breathing clean air, conserving nonrenewable resources, and reducing operational costs benefit everyone.
  • Find out if my local government has a citizens' committee whose focus is energy and/or the environment. Contact the committee to push to encourage finding solutions (such as Stealth Power, Vanner, and ZeroRPM) to idling of municipal vehicles, such as police cars and other emergency vehicles.
  • Use the materials from IdleBox to speak at my local civic association about the benefits of reducing unnecessary idling.
  • Talk to my local transit agency to propose ways of reducing unnecessary idling of buses and paratransit vehicles. Ideas in this National Center for Transportation Research report may be helpful.
  • Encourage fleet managers to contact their local Clean Cities Coordinator about ways to save money by reducing idling.
  • Consider registering as an EPA SmartWay partner or affiliate.

Hopefully, these pump primer resolutions will help guide what you decide to do.

Thanks for your past support in reducing unnecessary idling and spreading that message. We hope that you will continue to find our news and events useful to you in the coming year.

 

Terry M. Levinson, Editor
Allegheny Science & Technology
tlevinson@alleghenyst.com

 

Patricia Weikersheimer, Writer
Argonne National Laboratory
pweikersheimer@anl.gov

Electrified Parking Spaces

IdleAir Takes Over Operation of AireDock Units in Connecticut

IdleAir has assumed operations of the AireDock truck stop electrification (TSE) units on I-95 in Connecticut. IdleAir will offer free 120V power and heat through the end of February while the equipment is being upgraded. The participating I-95 locations include the northbound and southbound truck-stop sites in Darien, Fairfield, Branford, and Madison. According to an IdleAir press release, there is ample easy-in/easy-out angled parking with free WiFi and multiple restaurants available inside the rest-area building. More information, including videos, is available at IdleAir’s website.

 

IdleAir and eNow Partner To Offer Mutual Discounts

IdleAir, which provides stand-alone TSE services, and eNow, which provides solar-energy solutions for the transportation industry, have announced a strategic partnership. Each company is offering a 25% discount to customers who also use the services of the other company. eNow’s solar solutions for transportation will work with IdleAir’s grid-powered solutions to provide long-haul truckers an alternative to idling their engines during rest periods. For more information, please go to the website of PRWeb.

 

Other News of Interest

U.S. Air Force Studies Idling Reduction for Security-Force Sedans

The Air Force Security Forces Center is studying three idle-reduction systems at two installations: Joint Base San Antonio (Texas) and Offutt Air Force Base (Nebraska). Hot and cold weather, respectively, were the main factors for choosing these installations. The purpose of the study is to verify fuel savings, determine if the technologies are functional and reliable, validate the return-on-investment estimates, and study the initial effects on maintenance for security-force vehicles.

The Air Force Research Laboratory estimates that reducing the idling time by 70% would save $3,570 annually per sedan. Because the Air Force has more than 1,000 high-idle security-force vehicles, this represents considerable savings. Tech. Sgt. Kevin Moss, Program Branch Manager for the 441st Vehicle Support Chain Operations Squadron, which oversees the vehicle portion of the project, says, “We are hoping to reduce our impact on the environment, our miles per gallon and greenhouse gases. We figure a high-idling vehicle like this is going to produce more of those toxins and gases, so this would be a way to reduce our impact overall.” For more information about the study, please visit the Air Force Installation and Mission Support Center (AFIMSC) website.
