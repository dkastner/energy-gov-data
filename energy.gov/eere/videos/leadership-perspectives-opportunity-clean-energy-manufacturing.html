Leadership Perspectives: The Opportunity for Clean Energy Manufacturing

Addthis

Description 
There is a tremendous opportunity for the United States to manufacture clean energy and energy efficiency products. Leaders from U.S. industry and the U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) discuss the vision for a U.S. clean energy manufacturing future as well as factors that exist today that point to the potential for clean energy manufacturing growth in the United States. Watch this short video and learn more about these leaders' insights and DOE's Clean Energy Manufacturing Initiative.

Text Version

Below is the text version for the Leadership Perspectives: The Opportunity for Clean Energy Manufacturing video.

A lilting piano melody gently crescendos under David Danielson’s (Assistant Secretary for Energy Efficiency and Renewable Energy (EERE)) dialogue. We’re shown rows of azure-blue solar panels beneath a picturesque sky. Cumulus clouds churn and morph as the screen transitions to a lone windmill facing into a shrinking sunset. The blades spin in tempo to the music.

A man with gray hair points out something unseen on a laptop to two other individuals – one male in a dress shirt and one female in an electrical worker’s florescent jacket. Two of three wear hardhats.

The owner of the voice, David Danielson, appears before us against a jet black backdrop. His remarks are aimed at an audience slightly off screen.

The screen zooms in to an electronic prong moving rapidly over a small device, its internal metallic networks exposed. We transition rapidly to an orange torrent of flame and sparks cutting through a long ingot of metal. Rollers drag away the cut section. We catch a fleeting glimpse of an indeterminate manufacturing component illuminated in purple.

Caption: David Danielson - Assistant Secretary - EERE

David Danielson: "The Clean Energy Manufacturing Initiative was created to really rally the Department of Energy’s resources around the tremendous economic opportunity that stands before us in the manufacturing of clean energy and energy efficiency products."

Christopher Smith appears against a Jet Black Background.

Caption: Christopher Smith – Assistant Secretary - DOE Fossil Energy

Christopher Smith: Opportunities in terms of increased availability of efficient, low-cost feedstock in terms of natural gas. That’s creating opportunities throughout the manufacturing sector. We have great opportunities here in terms of reduced costs, lower price volatility—great opportunities to create jobs and create manufacturing opportunities here in the United States.

The video shows a several individuals working in a plant, with highly sophisticated turbines slowly spinning to life through Smith’s narration. A man uses a tool on a large cylinder. A large U.S. flag hangs as others work entering data or operating industrial equipment.  As the narrating voice changes, we’re shown a large suspended turbine. Mark Little (Senior Vice President (SVP) of General Electric) appears against a black background. A computer model of an energy technology is manipulated by an unseen user. A worker lowers a metal component into a larger machine.

Caption: Mark Little – SVP General Electric

Mark Little: At my time at GE, we spent our energies trying to find global sources for the commodities that built up our products. Now there’s a stream of innovation coming that’s taking us to a new place – and we’re actually insourcing and building manufacturing capabilities ourselves – it’s a whole new day.

Several individuals are shown around a table discussing a design. Their conversation is not audible. Hands are shown separating fibrous material. Workers intently focused on their task assemble engines with the assistance of robotic arms. Jean Redfield (CEO of NextEnergy) begins to narrate before she is pictured against a black background.

Caption: Jean Redfield – CEO NextEnergy

Jean Redfield: The way manufacturing works today is that nobody makes everything themselves. They all depend on a supply chain. What’s really important is that those regional, core investments in new technology, in workforce for the future, in infrastructure to link those new technologies and new talent into the existing supply chains. That’s the critical focus for U.S. competitiveness.

A worker hauls a large metal wheel with a pneumatic arm. Leo Gerard (President of United Steelworkers) appears against a black backdrop. A torrent of molten metal falls into a white hot pit. Our focus shifts to a video of planes, semi-trucks, and cargo ships.

Caption: Leo Gerard – President - United Steelworkers

Leo Gerard: Knowing from our own members in our union whether they’re in any of the manufacturing facilities, they can compete against anyone else in the world. And so it’s impossible to have a national manufacturing strategy if it’s not coordinated with a national skills strategy, if it’s not coordinated with a national strategy on exports.

Marty Durbin (Chief Executive Officer (CEO) of America’s Natural Gas Alliance) appears against a black background. A car production and testing plant shows footage from a robotic arm’s point of view. Hands handle metal components and a pickup truck hauling a black trailer speeds down a dirt road. Workers continue to maneuver components into place.

Caption: Marty Durbin – CEO - America’s Natural Gas Alliance

Marty Durbin: Competitiveness on productivity is what’s going to drive our economy going forward. It’s driving the increase in the number of manufacturing facilities here, the technology innovations that are coming out in power generation, in distributed generation, industrial and manufacturing applications, and transportation. Literally more than a hundred billion dollars in capital investments are already on the table.

Footage of computer modeling, microscopic procedures, and rapid 3D printing appear before Chuck Kahle(Chief Technology Officer (CTO) and Vice President (VP) of PPG Industries) can be seen against a jet black background.

Caption: Chuck Kahle CTO & VP – PPG Industries

Chuck Kahle: The changes in manufacturing that are occurring today, putting things like advanced manufacturing, 3D printing, mass customization, all of these things are accelerating. And you also see a change in the substrates and materials used for the manufacture of these products.

Machines continue to churn out delicate looking components. Curtis Carlson (CEO of SRI International) appears against a black backdrop. Dozens of solar panels are shown set up in a rural setting. Windmills accompany the solar panel array.

Caption: Curtis Carlson – CEO – SRI International

Curtis Carlson: Innovation is everything today. We’re in the – I call it – ‘the global innovation economy.’ The good news is it’s the best time for innovation ever. Every field is wide open. Manufacturing is one of the biggest changes we’ll ever see in our lifetime as well as all of the developments in energy.

Giant metal presses close around flaming industrial materials. A time lapse video of a 3D-printed car shows the car construction’s gradual progress. Then a team of people walk behind it as it is driven by two men.

David Danielson: The DOE plays a key role in helping bolster the United States’ energy innovation ecosystem. And our work has resulted in a wide variety of successes in advanced manufacturing and clean energy. And those advances have helped the United States become more competitive as a nation.

Wind turbines are assembled, with blades spinning in a variety of settings. National Renewable Energy Lab (NREL) and U.S. Department of Energy (DOE) logos are visible on some of the windmills.

Caption: Amy Ericson – U.S. President – Alstom

Amy Ericson: Alstom has been working with the DOE with 39 years. It helps us to accelerate through periods of uncertainty with technology. It helps us to bring technology to a point where we, as a private company, feel comfortable that we can offer commercial guarantees and take the financial risk.

A computer model of a jet and engine flies through black space.

Mark Little: We’re working with the national labs in a whole new way – finding ways to take advantage of their capabilities, and helping them to see how we can use their technologies most effectively at their end point in industry.

Giant objects are tested by being moved back and forth. Workers are seen focusing on various stages of manufacturing.

Caption: Jeff Fettig – Whirlpool Corporations

Jeff Fettig: Well I think it all starts with the competitiveness of our country. Ultimately, that’s the source of our ability to not only have a vibrant economy, but to export our products and compete around the world. Competitiveness, energy, innovation, infrastructure, these kind of things are critical for us to manage them in a new way to ensure that as a country we remain both competitive, but also drive economic growth.

Cars are seen moving along a busy stretch of road. Washington D.C. is pictured in the background. David Danielson appears against a black background to deliver his final comments.

David Danielson: If we’re successful, the Clean Energy Manufacturing Initiative is going to make us more competitive as a country. The shift to clean energy is going to occur over the next ten, twenty, thirty, forty years. So the investments we make in clean energy manufacturing competitiveness today will result in benefits for decades to come for this country.
