DOE Voluntary Protection Program Workshop - 32nd Annual National Voluntary Protection Programs Participants’ Association Conference

September 14, 2016 - 4:31pm

Addthis

A full house 120 DOE Federal and contractor personnel from across the DOE Complex attended the Office of Worker Safety and Health Assistance DOE VPP Pre-Conference Workshop on August 28.

Once again, the Office of Worker Safety and Health Assistance (AU-12) participated in the 32nd Annual National Voluntary Protection Programs Participants’ Association (VPPPA) conference held at the Gaylord Palms Resort and Conference Center in Kissimmee, Florida from August 28-September 1, 2016. This conference offers attendees a wide variety of workshops, meetings, and exhibitors all dedicated to providing a safe and healthy workplace. In an effort to capitalize on the Conference’s large DOE participation, AU-12 hosted a preconference workshop on Sunday, August 28, to provide DOE Voluntary Protection Program (VPP) participants and applicants with updates on the DOE-VPP. 120 DOE and DOE contractor personnel from across the DOE complex attended this year’s preconference workshop, up from just over 90 participants in 2015.

The preconference workshop began with an update from Brad Davy, the AU-12 Director, discussing upcoming changes to the DOE-VPP program, including working to add the documents to the DOE Technical Standards program. Brad also shared feedback on the annual submissions from DOE-VPP participants, with emphasis setting goals that reflect the emphasis on excellence. He encouraged all DOE participants to “shoot for the moon” in their safety programs, invoking President Kennedy’s 1963 challenge to land a man on the moon and return him safely before the end of the decade. The workshop continued with several DOE VPP participant prepared presentations. 

The rest of the morning was used by Los Alamos National Security, the operating contractor for the Los Alamos National Laboratory (LANL). The first presentation covered Los Alamos Operational Leadership efforts over the past several years, and included Rich Kacich, Deputy Director for the Los Alamos National Laboratory, William Mairson, Deputy Principal Associate Director of Operations and Business, and Michael Brandt, Associate Director for Environment, Safety, and Health.  The next presentation given by the LANL VPP office described Los Alamos’ efforts to implement Learning Teams as a means of improving corrective action development, and involve workers in identifying effective solutions to problems.

The next presentation, given by Cindy Lawton (MS, PT, OCS, BCPE, Physical Therapist, and a Board Certified Ergonomist) was well liked by the attendees. This presentation covered a proactive approach to ergonomics around the laboratory, and identified how investing in ergonomic improvements led to demonstrable savings and improved operational efficiency. The proactive approach helped Los Alamos reduce risk of injury, improve productivity, reduce error rate, and improve worker comfort.

The next presentation of the morning was given by Dena Edwards, Security Awareness Program Coordinator at Los Alamos. Dena described how Los Alamos has developed and deployed an active shooter training program that includes classroom training, walk-throughs, and realistic real time scenarios that allow workers to put their training into practice in a controlled environment. The award winning program has also reached out to groups in the community, including schools, churches, and financial institutions.

The final presentation from Los Alamos introduced LANL Radio, a clever new communication tool that uses music streaming technology to broadcast news, safety and security information and provide real-time updates in the event of an emergency. The tool was the brain-child of some Worker Safety and Security Team (WSST) members, and developed with cooperation from the communication staff. Although still in development, LANL Radio promises to be a “best practice” for communicating safety and security information.

After lunch, the sessions continued with a presentation from the Oak Ridge Institute for Science and Education (ORISE). ORISE presented its new VPPhD program that encourages personnel to complete a variety of safety education and improvement activities as they earn their Bachelors, Masters, and finally VPPhD.  ORISE developed this approach as a tool to counteract and prevent complacency about safety in a high performing organization, taking advantage of the academic leanings of much of the staff.

Our learning sessions concluded with a presentation from Washington River Protection Solutions (WRPS), the Tank Operations Contractor at the Hanford site. WRPS presented the background and results of workers asking a fundamental question that challenged a long-standing assumption. For many years, electricians working on the tank farm have had to choose between arc-flash protective equipment or respiratory equipment while working in areas that are prone to vapor release. Two years ago, an electrician questioned whether respirators provided adequate arc-flash protection. After testing, WRPS discovered the respirator mask did not provide sufficient protection. With assistance from the manufacturers, WRPS developed a modified arc flash visor that would protect a worker while wearing a respirator.

Following the educational portion of the program, AU recognized those participants that continue to demonstrate the continuing pursuit of excellence through VPP.  Each year, AU awards Superior Stars and Stars of Excellence to those participants that demonstrate excellence in outreach, mentoring, innovation, and the five tenets of VPP that lead to accident and injury rates significantly below their comparison industries. 5 participants achieved Superior Star, and 19 participants achieved the Star of Excellence. Three participants achieved the highest honor of “Legacy of Stars,” given in lieu of a fourth Star of Excellence in a row. Those three were Intermech, Inc. at the Hanford Waste Treatment Plant Construction Project, Mission Support Alliance Safeguards and Security at Hanford, and the Wastren Advantage, Inc. (WAI)) Hanford Laboratory.

This year AU-12 honored one DOE-VPP Contractor Champion.  Cynthia Caldwell from the Pacific Northwest National Laboratory (PNNL) was recognized for being the behind the scenes catalyst for the PNNL Voluntary Protection Program.  She assures employees receive the support needed to engage in the quest for excellence. Her untiring efforts to develop a culture of "yes we can make this a better work place" have spawned numerous improvements and innovative approaches to the safety and working environment at the laboratory. She supports individual contributors in their efforts to improve workspaces, safety committees in their efforts to address cross cutting issues, and garners managerial support for issues requiring their attention and involvement.  Cynthia is intimately involved in guaranteeing individuals opportunities to participate in regional and national VPP activities. Additionally, she has developed a predictive model for incidents and accidents that helps PNNL managers prioritize their safety improvement efforts.

On Sunday night, the VPPPA presented its annual awards.  These awards are presented for innovation, mentoring, outreach, and include scholarships for individuals pursuing degrees in safety and health-related fields. This year, 50 percent of the National VPPPA awards were given to DOE sites, demonstrating that DOE-VPP participants are truly leading the fight for safe and healthy workplaces. Winners included the aforementioned arc-flash protective shield.

The VPP Team dedicated the remainder of the week to attending a multitude of VPPPA workshops, networking between individuals and sites, and evaluating new ideas for potential application to other DOE sites. The AU-12 exhibit booth was once again a popular place for DOE attendees to meet, talk with the headquarters program staff, ask questions, identify potential issues, volunteer to participate in upcoming assessments, and share ideas.
