EM’s Award-Winning Projects Save More Than $10 Million, Advance Sustainability at Department of Energy

March 24, 2015 - 10:00am

Addthis

The SRS Non-Destructive Radionuclide Assay Team, from left, include Steve Mackmull (now retired), DOE-Savannah River; Todd Shepley, Canberra; and Lee Fox and Steve Mentrup, SRNS. Not pictured: Bert Crapse, DOE-Savannah River; Ronnie Lee, WIPP Central Characterization Program; and Dan Remington, Bruce Gillespie, and Joe Wachter, Canberra.

Teresa Krannig, UCOR’s IT project administrator, found ways to create savings that led UCOR to eliminate 58 printers and reduce toner cost from $125,000 to $32,400 annually.

WASHINGTON, D.C. – Two EM projects that spared resources, increased the efficiency of radioactive waste processing, and saved more than $10.6 million were honored as recipients of DOE’s 2014 Sustainability Awards.

   The Department highlighted the projects at its Savannah River Site (SRS) in South Carolina and Oak Ridge site in Tennessee as exceptional performance in sustainability. The annual awards program draws attention to DOE achievements that improve operations and save taxpayer money by reducing the Department’s waste and its use of energy and water while improving the energy efficiency of government buildings and fleet management.

   EM’s waste disposition organization at SRS and Savannah River Nuclear Solutions (SRNS) — EM’s management and operations contractor at SRS — worked with contractors Nuclear Waste Partnership (NWP) and Canberra Industries to develop a new non-destructive radionuclide assay technique that resulted in more than $10.5 million in cost savings in transuranic (TRU) waste processing operations.

   Now available to the entire DOE complex, the technique allowed the EM program at SRS to cut the amount of TRU waste requiring processing, repackaging, and transportation to EM’s Waste Isolation Pilot Plant (WIPP) for permanent disposal by 70 cubic meters. That amount would have filled 28 containers for shipment to WIPP. Under the technique, employees assayed the waste, or measured its level of radioactivity, and determined that the 70 cubic meters did not require shipment to WIPP.

   The program also avoided generating six cubic meters of secondary low-level waste for disposal at SRS, and the reduction in waste processing decreased the risk of worker hazards.

   NWP manages the WIPP Central Characterization Program, which is responsible for the waste characterization process at waste generator sites across the EM complex. Canberra is a subcontractor to NWP.

   “Extraordinary team effort leads to extraordinary results,” SRNS Solid Waste and F Area Operations Deputy Director Lee Fox said. “The process knowledge, industry expertise, and mutual cooperation made reaching this achievement possible. What we accomplished will have a significant impact on many organizations throughout the DOE complex.”

   EM’s Oak Ridge cleanup contractor, URS | CH2M Oak Ridge LLC (UCOR), created significant savings in an unlikely place.

   Away from the bulldozers, size compactors, and dump trucks, UCOR also oversees resources and business operations to support its activities at the East Tennessee Technology Park, Oak Ridge National Laboratory, and Y-12 National Security Complex. Information technology (IT) is one of the most crucial technical support functions for UCOR employees across the 33,500-acre Oak Ridge Reservation.

   Teresa Krannig, UCOR’s IT project administrator, found an opportunity to create savings and meet corporate efficiency goals. Krannig and her team accomplished this feat by implementing a managed print services concept, which required a transition from the company’s individually focused printer model.

   Originally, the company maintained more than 170 individual printers across the site; however, the change led to a managed print services contract and helped UCOR meet its sustainability goals. In the first year, UCOR deployed 34 percent fewer printers, used almost 500 fewer reams of paper, and reduced toner stock dramatically. The cost savings through toner and paper reduction alone totaled $92,600.

   With fewer printers, clicking the print button is no longer an automatic response for employees. The new approach reduced energy consumption by 14,000 kilowatts and resulted in 247,000 fewer pieces of copy paper being used from the previous year.

   “We are very proud of our contractor’s goals for greater efficiency, and employees, like Teresa, that identify solutions,” said Sue Cange, manager for the Oak Ridge Office of EM. “Aside from the environmental cleanup work they perform, UCOR is also working to limit future concerns.” 

   Through Krannig’s recommendation, UCOR has eliminated 58 printers and reduced toner cost from $125,000 to $32,400 annually. In addition, spent toner cartridges are skipping landfills and being returned to be recycled and remanufactured.
