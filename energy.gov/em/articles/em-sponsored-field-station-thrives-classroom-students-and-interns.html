EM-Sponsored Field Station Thrives as Classroom for Students and Interns

September 2, 2015 - 12:00pm

Addthis

Akela Scott (left) and Taurean Houston (center), both seniors at South Carolina State University, explain their findings on species richness at SRS to Dr. Trina Halfhide, an instructor in environmental sciences from the University of the West Indies, St. Augustine, Trinidad and Tobago campus.

AIKEN, S.C. – The Savannah River Site’s (SRS) ecosystem, atmosphere, and groundwater provide unique classrooms, rich in data and samples and compelling enough to entice young minds to science careers.

The nearly 20-year-old Savannah River Environmental Sciences Field Station (SRESFS), sponsored by EM and guided by scientists and researchers at EM’s Savannah River National Laboratory (SRNL), is thriving as a field station for predominantly minority-based undergraduate students who benefit from a focus on applied research and integrating curriculum with hands-on field work.

Twenty-six students and interns enrolled in Orangeburg’s South Carolina State University (SCSU) or another Historically Black College or University (HBCU), spent this summer at SRNL in a two-level program that starts with coursework training for undergraduate students and ends with a research emphasis for interns who have returned to SRESFS from previous years to work at SRNL. This year, 21 students were in the course track, and five interns participated in research.

Highlights of their summer studies were featured in a student and intern poster symposium held recently at SRNL. In a meeting room full of SRNL scientists and researchers, SCSU seniors Akela Scott, an industrial technology and technology education major, and Taurean Houston, a mechanical engineering technology major, turned a back corner of the room into a project recap of their summer project for the many attendees who came their way. The two teamed in May to learn about species richness at SRS, studying the marsh rabbit as the reference species.

In their research, Scott and Houston found that SRS had a high species richness and density, particularly in the southwestern edge of the site and along the water-rich banks of Upper Three Runs, Par Pond, and other bodies of water. These findings onsite mimic trends statewide, where species richness is most dense along the state’s coastal edge, near to lakes and streams, and at the northern border that sits at the foothills of the Blue Ridge Mountains in North Carolina. They found that the high species richness at SRS may be attributed to the proximity of large bodies of water and the amount of conservation efforts and limited urban development.

“We spent the last two months building up to this,” said Scott. “It’s been summer school, and it’s been learning. It has opened our eyes.”

Houston added, “This gave us an in-depth view about what affects the environment, both in recent and long-term impacts.”

Tyrone Bookard, a mechanical engineering technology undergraduate, and Darius Priester, a Master of Business Administration candidate at SCSU, also were on a mentored research tract of the SRESFS program that looked at the geochemical spatial analysis of the groundwater system at SRS. In their research, the team collected groundwater samples from across SRS. They found that the groundwater quality index at background wells installed in the Gordon Aquifer was of excellent quality by World Health Organization guidelines.

Dr. Candice Simmons, acting director of SRESFS, said the summer program is the only undergraduate program of its kind in the country that recruits minority students. She has seen firsthand the impact of this summer field experience. Often, she said, the students don’t know these types of environmental jobs exist.

“It certainly is creating awareness,” she said. “There’s a gap there, and this program provides exposure.”

To date, SRESFS has provided internships to more than 450 students in 19 years. The program is funded and supported by EM and managed and operated by SCSU. Additional facilities and support are given by the DOE Savannah River Operations Office, the University of Georgia’s Savannah River Ecology Laboratory at SRS, and the U.S. Forest Service Savannah River Operations Office.
