7th Workshop on Risk Informed Regulation and Safety Culture

May 19, 2015 - 5:51pm

Addthis

Sarah Lennon at the Qinshann Nuclear Power Plan Museum

Sarah Lennon, Acting Director of the Office of Bilateral Cooperation attended the 7th Workshop on Risk Informed Regulation and Safety Culture held in Shenzhen, China, from March 23-27. More than 70 Chinese participants attended the Workshop, the main purpose of which was to take a multidisciplinary approach, using academic/theoretical, industrial practices, and regulatory perspectives to look at risks existing in nuclear power plants (NPPs). This series of workshops designed by the Office of Nuclear Energy in concert with experts from the Idaho National Laboratory are designed to help China develop a comprehensive, successful and efficient Risk-Informed Regulatory framework. Nearly all the participants were under the age of 35, from 28 different nuclear power plants, research and design institutes, universities, nuclear power plant operating companies, nuclear safety organizations, and government entities.

She offered opening remarks for the U.S. delegation that focused on our partnership with China on safety issues and their increasing role in the global nuclear energy market; one individual commented that her remarks were ”inspiring.” The U.S. delegation was hosted for dinner the first night by the China General Nuclear organization and SNPI, and it was a feast for the eyes as well as the palate! More than 20 different dishes, Cantonese family style in a relaxed and beautiful environment that allowed the two delegations to get to know each other better and to make more personal connections.

What was the most fascinating about the workshop was the innovative work that is being done by academics at the University of Illinois-Urbana Champagne, in concert with industry (the South Texas Project), DOE national laboratory (Argonne National Laboratory) and regulators. They talked about the need to make PSA plant specific, conducting modeling to take into consideration human sociology, culture, geography, environment – it was not a “one size fits all” situation. They also demonstrated that implementing Probabilistic Safety Assessments (PSA) can actually benefit a facility’s bottom line, rather than simply being an imposition from a regulatory authority. The lead researcher reiterated that each plant needed to “make it your own” and not blindly copy what the United States does.

She also had the opportunity to visit the Qinshan Nuclear Power Station, a multi-unit plant located in Zhejiang Province ~ 60 miles southwest of Shanghai; it is the load center of the East China Grid. She was hosted by the CNNP Nuclear Power Operations Management Co., Ltd, a subsidiary of the China National Nuclear Company (CNNC). Qinshan considers itself the birth place of the nuclear power industry of China. The hosts proudly highlighted that they had won the prestigious “Pride of China” award a number of years ago and their briefing noted that the plant was “built with China’s own strength.” It has a total of 9 units (1 300MWe PWR; 2 1,000MWe; 4 650MWe; and 2 700MWe Candu-6) in operation. It is stunning to learn how fast Qinshan has grown from an initial one unit (300MWe) visited by DOE Undersecretary Card in 2001 to the current status.

Qinshan has developed its own Probabilistic Risk Assessments (PRA), unlike other utilities relying on other research institutes to support their PRA. However, Qinshan will hire two U.S. PRA firms to develop its “Generation Risk Analysis” model. This summer, a team of Qinshan PSA personnel will visit South Texas Project Operating Company (STP) to learn how STP developed its PSA software and applications. Their attention to safety was noteworthy. Every day they begin their meetings with a safety announcement a timely topic, and the plant museum has beautiful and targeted displays related to safety and nuclear power.
Ms. Lennon was impressed with the passion of the Chinese presenters and the progress that has been made since DOE/NE first started the PSA workshops. SNPI wants to be a national leader in PRA and DOE’s goal is to help them develop the capability to train and educate China’s operators and regulators into the future. DOE’s go-to guy who organized the workshop on the Chinese side said, at the beginning of the Workshop, that he thought “the future is in this room,” and if that is the case, then DOE/NE’s mission will have been successful.
