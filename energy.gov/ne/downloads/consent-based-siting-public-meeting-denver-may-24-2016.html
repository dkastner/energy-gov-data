Consent-Based Siting Public Meeting in Denver (May 24, 2016)

Consent-Based Siting Public Meeting in Denver

Meeting Summary

On May 24, 2016, the Department of Energy’s consent-based siting initiative hosted its fourth public meeting in Denver, Colorado at the Embassy Suites – Stapleton. The purpose of this meeting was to hear from the public and stakeholders on important elements in the design of a consent-based siting process. A consent-based siting process will support the development of facilities needed to manage spent nuclear fuel and high-level radioactive waste, including consolidated interim storage facilities and permanent geologic repositories. 

The agenda included a presentation from the Department of Energy’s Acting Assistant Secretary for Nuclear Energy, John Kotek. Mr. Kotek discussed the nuclear energy activities that have brought us to this point, as well as described the Department’s vision for an integrated waste management system and the need for a consent-based approach to siting. This presentation was followed by a panel session with several experts providing diverse perspectives on the primary issues that need to be resolved in the design and implementation of a consent-based process. Participants then had the opportunity to comment or ask questions to the Department and the panelists.

Following this session, participants engaged in facilitated small group discussions on a variety of topics related to consent-based siting and integrated waste management. These small group discussions provided the opportunity for frank and open conversations on key topics that will inform the design of a consent-based process.

The agenda also included a public comment period and two open houses with poster sessions before and after the formal meeting. The open house sessions provided participants with the opportunity to engage in less formal discussions with the Department and other meeting attendees.

Approximately 50 members of the public attended the meeting in person and 20 participants viewed the meeting via webinar.

Keynote and Panel

The meeting began with opening remarks by Michael Sullivan, former Governor of Wyoming. Governor Sullivan’s address focused on his experience with the Department’s Monitored Retrievable Storage (MRS) program in the 1980s and 1990s and how that effort may be relevant to today’s consent-based siting initiative. In particular, the Governor reviewed a 1992 letter he wrote to Wyoming’s Fremont County Commissioners in which he withdrew state support from further engagement with the Department regarding the proposed MRS facility. In this letter, the Governor questioned the Department’s overall waste management policy at the time; cited the lack of trust in the federal government’s ability to follow through on commitments; and reflected on the relative ability of Wyoming and its citizens to effectively engage with the federal government over a protracted discernment and evaluation process. Governor Sullivan then contrasted this experience from the early 1990s to the Department’s current siting program. To that end, he recommended that the federal government undertake a comprehensive awareness-building initiative so that the problem being resolved is better appreciated by a wider audience. The Governor then emphasized the inherent uncertainties involved in nuclear waste management including political and legislative changes. Finally, Governor Sullivan reinforced the capacity theme from his 1992 letter and emphasized the level of commitment, resources, and time that would be required for any community to make an informed and durable decision to host a waste management facility.

Governor Sullivan’s comments were followed by a presentation from John Kotek, Acting Assistant Secretary for Nuclear Energy. Mr. Kotek’s presentation began with a brief video recording from Secretary of Energy Moniz who emphasized the importance of finding a lasting nuclear waste management solution and how such an effort must be grounded in a consent-based siting process. Mr. Kotek then described the Administration’s integrated waste management strategy as well as reviewed the Department’s approach to developing a consent-based siting process. A copy of Mr. Kotek’s presentation can be found at the bottom of this page.

Following Mr. Kotek’s remarks, the panelists provided their perspectives on consent-based siting and the issues the Department should consider as it moves forward.  Panel members included:

  • Maury Galbraith, Executive Director, Western Interstate Energy Board
  • Don Hancock, Southwest Research Information Center
  • Sally Jameson, National Conference of State Legislatures
  • Mervyn Tano, International Institute for Indigenous Resource Management

Mr. Galbraith focused on a series of siting process considerations ranging from waste transportation and route selection to the issue of competing interests and values and their influence on siting efforts.  From a transportation perspective, Mr. Galbraith discussed the risk management and safety concerns expressed by those stakeholders adjacent to routes along which nuclear materials may pass. In particular, he discussed the issue of consent that these stakeholders may or may not have given to the transportation of nuclear materials and that how working with them as an active and meaningful participant in the process would serve to strengthen the overall siting effort. Mr. Galbraith then discussed how various interests and perspectives contribute to facility siting, and in particular, how it may not be possible to develop a solution that satisfies everybody’s interests but that the keys to a lasting solution are understanding others’ values, transparency, equity, and mutual trust between the parties.

Following Mr. Galbraith’s remarks, Mr. Hancock offered his thoughts and perspectives. He challenged the Department’s emphasis on consent-based siting, stating that the issue of consent is poorly defined and is not the “law of the land.” Mr. Hancock further emphasized his point that consent has not been sought or obtained in previous Departmental siting programs and that this lack of experience is the cause for a degree of stakeholder skepticism and caution. Mr. Hancock then discussed the quantities of nuclear materials that need to be managed and expressed concern that without a better definition of the problem and the proposed solution, the capacity of the proposed storage facilities may grow over time, leaving potential host communities with a greater burden than the one to which they originally agreed.

Sally Jameson’s observations included perspectives as a Maryland resident living near the Calvert Cliffs Nuclear Power Plant, as well as her role as Chair of the National Conference of State Legislature's (NCSL) Nuclear Legislative Workgroup. With respect to Calvert Cliffs, Ms. Jameson emphasized the high level of engagement between the plant and the surrounding communities regarding the operation of the plant and ongoing fuel storage matters and how this engagement has served to build a threshold level of trust between stakeholders and the utility. In particular, Ms. Jameson emphasized the importance of state regulators and legislators in this discussion and how they play a key role in building a common understanding. Accordingly, she recommended that the Department use this engagement model as a reference point for the larger conversation with respect to consent-based siting. From her perspective as a member of NCSL, Ms. Jameson discussed the overall scope of the nuclear waste management problem and how, as a nation, the country must move forward to collectively solve the problem. In doing so, Ms. Jameson reiterated the need to work closely with national, tribal, state, regional, and local stakeholders to develop a common understanding of the nature of the problem and the range of proposed solutions.

The final panel member to speak was Mr. Mervyn Tano from the International Institute for Indigenous Resource Management. In his remarks, Mr. Tano used the symbol of a cake as a metaphor for siting nuclear facilities. In this case, he described the Department’s offerings (of a waste management facility) as ready-made cake, complete with frosting and decorations with the key issue being that all decisions about the cake have been made—that no other cake was available and that other options (frosting, decoration, etc.) were not negotiable. Mr. Tano’s example was used to emphasize that, in his opinion, the Department would take the same approach to a nuclear waste management facility and prescribe all the criteria for the facility siting, design, construction, and operation with no input from those who would be hosts. Such an approach, per Mr. Tano, would not be effective and would erode the Department’s credibility. With respect to the issue of consent, Mr. Tano then described how “free, prior and informed consent,” as outlined in United Nation’s Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNRIP), may provide a useful framework for working with interested communities. An excerpt from the UNRIP, adopted in 2007, follows:

"States shall consult and cooperate in good faith with the Indigenous Peoples concerned through their own representative institutions in order to obtain their free, prior and informed consent before adopting and implementing legislative or administrative measures that may affect them.”

Facilitated Question and Answer Session

Following the panel presentation, meeting participants asked questions to the panel members (including Mr. Kotek). Panel members engaged with the participants on several issues ranging from the importance of transparent engagement to factors that may influence the ability of stakeholders to effectively participate in siting deliberations. Additional issues that were discussed included the relative safety of the Rocky Flats weapons complex, transportation risks, the proposed timeframe associated with interim storage, the experiences of nuclear waste management in other countries, and the next steps the Department plans on taking as part of its consent-based siting process.  

Facilitated Small Group Discussions

After a short break, meeting participants were invited to join in facilitated small group discussions to explore the issues involved in consent-based siting. These discussions were facilitated by independent third-party professional facilitators. Participants formed five small groups, each consisting of six to ten members of the public. In addition, each group included a note taker and a Department representative.  The groups met for approximately one hour and discussed a wide range of issues associated with developing a consent-based siting process. At the end of the one-hour discussion, the facilitator from each table highlighted his or her group’s discussion and reported key observations back to the larger meeting. 

Key issues discussed during these small group discussions included, but are not limited to:

  • Risks and Benefits – The need for a comprehensive understanding of the risks and benefits posed by the current nuclear waste management situation and how that is proposed to change as a result of the Department’s policies.
  • New Organization – The need for a new organization, relatively unencumbered by political constraints, to lead the nation’s nuclear waste management efforts.
  • Informed Consent – The nature of informed consent, how it is defined, by whom and under what conditions. Access to resources is an important component to enable communities to participate in the consent-based siting process.
  • Local/Regional Control – The clear and important role of local, regional, state, and tribal entities in the consent-based siting effort and how these stakeholders may be integrated into the Department’s initiative.

A summary report of these small group discussions is included at the bottom of the page.

Public Comment Period

Following the small group discussions, a 30-minute comment period took place to allow members of the public to make statements. These statements included but were not limited to the need for increased attention to legacy sites related to the U.S. nuclear program (abandoned uranium production), environmental issues at Rocky Flats, the need for robust and patient stakeholder dialogue, and the requirement for transparency in all the Department’s actions. Additional statements focused on the low carbon nature of nuclear power and its value in a carbon-constrained economy, the relative safety of stored spent nuclear fuel, historic practices at Rocky Flats and the significant resource and capacity requirements of potentially interested host communities to engage with the Department. There were also advocates of a thorium based fuel cycle who stated the technology has fewer safety risks and produces less of a waste stream.

Closing Remarks

Following the public comment period, Mr. Andrew Griffith, Associate Deputy Assistant Secretary for Fuel Cycle Technologies, offered closing remarks in which he thanked the audience for their active and thoughtful participation and reinforced the Department’s commitment to stakeholder engagement as it moves forward with the consent-based siting process.

Thank you to all who participated!
