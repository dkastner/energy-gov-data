Consent-Based Siting Public Meeting in Sacramento (April 26, 2016)

Consent-Based Siting Public Meeting in Sacramento

Meeting Summary

On April 26, 2016, the Department of Energy’s consent-based siting initiative hosted its third public meeting in Sacramento, California at the Holiday Inn Capitol Plaza. The purpose of this meeting was to hear from the public and stakeholders on important elements in the design of a consent-based siting process. A consent-based siting process will support the development of facilities needed to manage spent nuclear fuel and high-level radioactive waste, including consolidated interim storage facilities and permanent geologic repositories. 

The agenda included a presentation from the Department of Energy’s Acting Assistant Secretary for Nuclear Energy, John Kotek. Mr. Kotek discussed the nuclear energy activities that have brought us to this point, as well as described the Department’s vision for an integrated waste management system and the need for a consent-based approach to siting. This presentation was followed by a panel session with several experts providing diverse perspectives on the primary issues that need to be resolved in the design and implementation of a consent-based process. Participants then had the opportunity to comment or ask questions to the Department and the panelists.

Following this session, participants engaged in facilitated small group discussions on a variety of topics related to consent-based siting and integrated waste management. These small group discussions provided the opportunity for frank and open conversations on key topics that will inform the design of a consent-based process.

The agenda also included a public comment period and two open houses with poster sessions before and after the formal meeting. The open house sessions provided participants with the opportunity to engage in less formal discussions with the Department and other meeting attendees.

Approximately 50 members of the public attended the meeting in person and 30 participants viewed the meeting via webinar.

Keynote and Panel

The meeting began with opening remarks by Robert Weisenmiller, Chairman of the California Energy Commission. Chairman Weisenmiller described the state of nuclear waste storage in California and the storage of waste in communities that have not granted consent to act as long-term hosts of that waste. He urged the Department to act expeditiously to seek voluntary storage and disposal facilities away from California’s coastal regions. With respect to the siting process, Chairman Weisenmiller emphasized the importance of a transparent and inclusive public process that builds trust between all parties and fully addresses transportation considerations. Finally, Chairman Weisenmiller stressed the importance of providing financial and technical resources to interested communities to allow them to fully and equitably participate in the consent-based siting process.

Chairman Weisenmiller’s comments were followed by a presentation from John Kotek, Acting Assistant Secretary for Nuclear Energy.  Mr. Kotek’s presentation began with a brief video recording from Secretary of Energy Dr. Ernest Moniz who emphasized the importance of finding a lasting nuclear waste management solution and how such an effort must be grounded in a consent-based siting process. Mr. Kotek then described the Administration’s integrated waste management strategy as well as reviewed the Department’s approach to developing a consent-based siting process. A copy of Mr. Kotek’s presentation can be found at the bottom of this page.

Following Mr. Kotek’s remarks, the panelists provided their perspectives on consent-based siting and the issues the Department should consider as it moves forward. Panel members included:

  • James David Ballard, Ph.D., Professor of Sociology, California State University Northridge
  • Rochelle Becker, Executive Director, Alliance for Nuclear Responsibility
  • Gabriel Bohnee, Director of Environmental Restoration & Waste Management, Nez Perce Tribe
  • Daniel Stetson, Executive Director, The Nicholas Endowment

Professor Ballard focused his discussion on the ethics, politics, and socio-economic issues related to consent-based siting. His remarks were grouped around “four Ts:” transportation, terrorism, tribal, and trust. With respect to transportation, Professor Ballard focused on the need to engage with stakeholders along routes as part of the planning and risk management process. He then described terrorism in relation to nuclear materials as “human initiated events” and discussed how the government should enhance the security of the waste materials by proactively anticipating risks and developing response plans. Professor Ballard then moved to address tribal issues associated with transportation and disposal and the unique role that Tribal Nations play in large-scale land use decisions. The matter of trust, or lack thereof, between the various parties and the Department of Energy was the final element of Professor Ballard’s statements and he urged the Department to recognize and overcome the existing trust deficit on the path to a sustainable solution. As one way to enhance the credibility of the consent-based process, Dr. Ballard suggested that the Department retain an independent group of external experts to provide objective information to stakeholders throughout the siting program.

Following Professor Ballard’s remarks, Ms. Rochelle Becker offered her thoughts and perspectives. She emphasized that California’s nuclear wastes are in seismically active coastal zones and that this was a sub-optimal situation. Echoing Dr. Ballard’s statements, Ms. Becker reiterated the lack of trust in the Department and suggested that a new agency, if properly structured, would be able to act in a manner that may be seen as more trustworthy and build greater success in siting process outcomes.  Furthermore, Dr. Ballard suggested that this new agency should not have a role in promoting nuclear power (as does the Department) and that by separating the promotion of nuclear power from the need to find a nuclear waste solution, the new agency would be able to constructively engage with more stakeholders. Ms. Becker then discussed the need for stakeholders to gain access to trusted information and recommended the creation of an independent group of experts to provide this ongoing resource.  With access to this information resource, Ms. Becker felt that stakeholders from diverse backgrounds and interests may more effectively engage with each other to seek lasting solutions.

Gabriel Bohnee then addressed the siting of nuclear waste facilities from his perspective as a member of the Nez Perce Tribe. Mr. Bohnee’s remarks focused on the far-reaching influence of tribal entities and how this influence spans an area greater than official land boundaries. For instance, while the Nez Perce reservation is located in Western Idaho, its influence ranges from the Pacific Ocean to Montana. Accordingly, as the Department begins to design its consent-based siting process, it needs to consider this aspect of tribal influence in its engagement and consultation process. Furthermore, given that there are more than 500 federally recognized Indian tribes, this matter requires careful planning and inclusive implementation. Mr. Bohnee also discussed the participation barriers that tribal entities may encounter and how access to resources will improve both the engagement process and the final outcome.

The final panel member to speak was Mr. Dan Stetson from the Nicholas Endowment who is also a member of San Onofre’s Community Engagement Panel (CEP). Mr. Stetson’s talk began with a discussion of safety: namely that the safety of the population, local community, and environment should be the focus of any nuclear waste management effort. From his role with the CEP, Mr. Stetson felt that moving the spent fuel away from the San Onofre community was of paramount importance but he did want to unduly burden a potential host community. To that end, Mr. Stetson recommended that there be a strong and clear value proposition offered to host communities as part of the siting process and that each potentially interested community be provided with resources necessary to arrive at their own decision. If such a process was developed, Mr. Stetson felt that communities may seek to compete for hosting a site and that this development would encourage transparency in decision-making. Mr. Stetson also focused on the common theme of trust and suggested that the CEP’s model of engagement could be replicated and used by the Department in the consent-based siting process. However, one limitation noted by Mr. Stetson was that the current CEP does not represent a diversity of socio-economic perspectives and that going forward, efforts should be made to make this, and other similar panels, more representative.

Facilitated Question and Answer Session

Following the panel presentation, meeting participants asked questions to the panel members (including Mr. Kotek). Panel members engaged with the participants on several issues ranging from the importance of transparent stakeholder engagement to societal considerations that may influence the ability of stakeholders to effectively participate in siting deliberations. Additional issues included safety and transportation risks, the proposed timeframe associated with interim storage, the experiences of nuclear waste management in other countries, and the next steps the Department plans on taking as part of its consent-based siting process.  

Facilitated Small Group Discussions

After a short break, meeting participants were invited to join in facilitated small group discussions to explore the issues involved in consent-based siting. These discussions were facilitated by independent third-party professional facilitators. Participants formed five small groups, each consisting of six to ten members of the public. In addition, each group included a note taker and a Department representative.  The groups met for approximately one hour and discussed a wide range of issues associated with developing a consent-based siting process. At the end of the one-hour discussion, the facilitator from each table highlighted his or her group’s discussion and reported key observations back to the larger meeting. 

Key issues discussed during these small group discussions included, but are not limited to:

  • Consent – The phased nature of consent, how it is earned or granted over time, and how consent needs to be maintained throughout the siting and operation of a potential facility.
  • Process and timing – Understanding the siting process, the steps in the process, milestones, and decision points.  Having this process appreciated early on would be beneficial and would assist in managing expectations.
  • Siting criteria and standards – The nature of technical, safety, process, and other siting criteria need to be fully recognized by the parties to better enable constructive engagement.
  • Inclusivity – Ensure that all parties are identified, present, and sufficiently resourced to allow for effective participation. It is important to identify who needs to be involved and how to best engage with them, as well as respond to stakeholders’ varying needs for how information is disseminated and internalized.

A summary report of these small group discussions is included at the bottom of the page.

Public Comment Period

Following the small group discussions, a 30-minute comment period took place to allow members of the public to make statements. These statements included but were not limited to topics such as the need for a broad public outreach and awareness effort, the importance of diverse representation in public deliberation and the underlying need for access to information that is readily understandable.  Additional statements included expressions of mistrust in the Department of Energy, questions about the need for storage facilities, emphasis on the importance of nuclear power in the context of climate change, noting the parallels between consent-based siting and informed consent in the medical profession, and the need for a better appreciation for the risks involved with moving fuel.

Closing Remarks

Following the public comment period, Mr. Andrew Griffith, Associate Deputy Assistant Secretary for Fuel Cycle Technologies, offered closing remarks in which he thanked the audience for their active and thoughtful participation and reinforced the Department’s commitment to stakeholder engagement as it moves forward with the consent-based siting process.

Thank you to all who participated!
