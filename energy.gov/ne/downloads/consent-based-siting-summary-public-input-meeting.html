Consent-Based Siting Summary of Public Input Meeting

Consent-Based Siting Summary of Public Input Meeting

Meeting Summary

The Department recently concluded the initial phase of public engagement on consent-based siting, which began with an Invitation for Public Comment in the Federal Register on December 23, 2015. The consent-based siting initiative received more than 10,000 comments in response to the Invitation for Public Comment in the Federal Register requesting feedback from communities, states, Tribal Nations, and interested stakeholders on important considerations in designing a fair and effective process. The Department also hosted eight public meetings across the United States in 2016 to get public input on the elements that should be considered in the development of a consent-based siting process.

On September 15, 2016, the Department hosted a public meeting to summarize the input received and discuss next steps in designing a consent-based siting process. The meeting took place in Washington, DC at the Embassy Suites Convention Center. At this meeting, the Department also presented the draft report, Designing a Consent-Based Siting Process: Summary of Public Input.

A transcript, agenda, speaker biographies, and the draft Summay of Public Input Report are included at the bottom of this page.

Panel

The meeting began with a presentation from the Department of Energy’s Acting Assistant Secretary for Nuclear Energy John Kotek. Mr. Kotek summarized the Department’s stakeholder outreach program, the purpose of which is to engage with the American public to listen to their thoughts regarding the consent-based siting of nuclear waste management facilities. This outreach effort began on December 23, 2015 with the publication of a Request for Information in the Federal Register inviting communities, states, Tribes and other interested parties to offer their thoughts on the design of a consent-based siting process. More specifically, this notice included the following five questions to help guide responses:

  • How can the Department of Energy ensure that the process for selecting a site is fair?
  • What models and experience should the Department of Energy use in designing the process?
  • Who should be involved in the process for selecting a site, and what is their role?
  • What information and resources do you think would facilitate your participation?
  • What else should be considered? 

Mr. Kotek also described the eight public meetings the Department hosted around the country in 2016 (in Chicago, Atlanta, Sacramento, Denver, Boston, Tempe, Boise, and Minneapolis) to increase the dialogue and further engage with the public. More than 600 members of the public participated in the meetings in person and via webinar. The meetings included discussion from 40 panel members, each expressing diverse views, and facilitated small group discussions to foster more in-depth discussions among meeting participants. These meetings provided the Department with a large volume of input which informed the draft report, Designing a Consent-Based Siting Process: Summary of Public Input. Kotek concluded his remarks with a discussion of the pending plans to re-organize the Office of Nuclear Energy within the Department of Energy to improve its ability to support a consent-based siting program and position it to better transition to another entity in the event of authorizing legislation.

Following Mr. Kotek’s presentation, Acting Team Lead for the Nuclear Fuels Storage and Transportation Planning Project Melissa Bates summarized the public comments as reflected in the draft report, Designing a Consent-Based Siting Process: Summary of Public Input. Ms. Bates began her remarks by emphasizing the importance of public engagement in designing an effective siting process and thanking those members of the public who participated in the public meetings and engaged with the Department. She acknowledged the time and effort that people have given to engage and provide comments and thanked them for doing so. With respect to the main themes from the public engagement, Ms. Bates’ summary included public comments pertaining to:

  • Lack of trust in the Department to implement a siting process that builds public confidence.
  • A desire for a new agency to be responsible for U.S. nuclear waste management efforts.
  • The importance of considering the needs of those communities who presently host nuclear waste from shutdown civilian nuclear reactors who have not agreed to host spent nuclear fuel for the long term.
  • Given that nuclear materials remain radioactive of hundreds of thousands of years, the need for the Department to be sensitive to fairness to future generations in the design and implementation of a consent-based siting process.
  • The importance of environmental justice considerations.
  • The recommendation to include the needs of transportation stakeholders into Departmental decision-making and actions.
  • The unique relationship Tribes have with the federal government and the importance of Tribal sovereignty in decisions regarding siting of nuclear waste management facilities.
  • The multidisciplinary nature of the conversations regarding consent-based siting (technical, cultural, socioeconomic, political) and the need to consider a diversity of viewpoints in siting process development and implementation.
  • The need to include a wide variety of stakeholders at the local, regional, state, tribal, and federal levels, along with sufficient resources to allow for their effective participation.
  • The need for clear siting standards along with a transparent process for developing standards.
  • The desire for greater specificity as to how “consent” may be defined, by whom, and the significance of a stakeholder community voicing “non-consent” with respect to siting.

Ms. Bates concluded by reinforcing her appreciation for the honest and forthright nature of public comments and expressed optimism that, through continued dialogue and engagement, pathway may be developed for the siting of nuclear waste management facilities.

Following Ms. Bates’ comments, Associate Deputy Assistant Secretary for Fuel Cycle Technologies Andrew Griffith concluded the panel presentation by discussing the Department’s future nuclear waste management plans. First, the Department will be accepting public comment on the draft report, Designing a Consent-Based Siting Process: Summary of Public Input,  through October 30, 2016 and will issue a final report that summarizes public input by December 2016.

With respect to the design of a consent-based siting process, Mr. Griffith referenced that a draft report of the Department’s preliminary siting process would be issued for public comment by the end of the December 2016. The Department will incorporate input received during the initial engagement efforts in this process. This report will not be final, but will be released in draft form to solicit public comment and input on siting process design.  

Mr. Griffith also noted that the Department recognizes the importance of active and informed communities. In order to support community engagement, the Department has requested $25M in its FY 2017 budget to fund interested communities to seek information on consent-based siting. Approval for this funding request is pending.

Mr. Griffith also discussed activities to advance the different components of an integrated waste management system, including transportation, storage, and disposal capabilities. Regarding transportation, the Department continues to work with State and Regional Groups regarding plans to move spent nuclear fuel and high-level nuclear waste, as well as assess transportation infrastructure needs.. Mr. Griffith also referenced the Department’s work on the Atlas railcar, which is currently under development to provide safe and efficient transportation of spent nuclear fuel in the future.

Mr. Griffith then discussed the Department’s efforts to develop the consolidated interim storage concept through generic design safety analysis activities. The Department is also interested in learning more about self-financed, private initiatives and how they might fit into the Department’s waste management plants. To that end, the Department intends to issue a Request for Information in the coming months to invite interested parties to offer their perspectives on this matter.

Finally, Mr. Griffith discussed the Department’s activities related to disposal. The Department continues to evaluate disposal in three main geology types: crystalline, clay/shale, and salt. Mr. Griffith also discussed the Department’s deep borehole efforts. Mr. Griffith reviewed the current procurement to conduct a field test to evaluate the feasibility of the deep borehole method. Mr. Griffith discussed the recent attempt to conduct the field test, which met with local opposition and resulted in a new procurement that emphasizes public engagement and collaboration. Mr. Griffith also discussed the Department’s work in the area of a repository for high-level radioactive waste resulting from defense activities. The Department will issue a draft plan intended to initiate public input and feedback on how the Department may proceed with a repository for defense waste that “not only makes sense technically but is part of the consent-based process.”

Facilitated Question and Answer Session

Following the panel presentation, meeting participants (both meeting attendees and webinar participants) asked questions to the panel members on several issues. These included, but were not limited to: the role of Yucca Mountain in the department’s nuclear waste management plans, the means by which the Department will measure or evaluate “consent” as part of the siting process, the need to engage with communities who currently host spent nuclear fuel, and the timing of Office of Nuclear Energy’s pending reorganization. Additional questions referenced transportation planning efforts, the need for a new agency (separate from the Department) to focus exclusively on nuclear waste management, and environmental justice considerations in the consent-based siting process.

Closing Remarks

Following the Question and Answer session, Mr. John Kotek offered closing remarks by stressing the importance of moving forward on this issue and the value of public engagement in building an effective consent-based siting process. Mr. Kotek concluded his remarks by thanking the audience for their participation and by expressing his gratitude for the work performed by the Department’s consent-based siting team.
