Small Business Program Extends Hand to Vendors, Entrepreneurs Throughout Idaho

January 7, 2015 - 1:47pm

Addthis

The U.S. Department of Energy’s Idaho National Laboratory reached out to small business owners throughout the state recently by meeting individually and in groups to explain the national laboratory’s needs for achieving its research missions.

Nearly 50 people participated in the lab’s Small Business Forum Nov. 13 at INL’s new gateway research center, the Energy Innovation Laboratory. The forum was hosted by the Department of Energy-Idaho Operations Office, Idaho National Laboratory, Idaho Cleanup Project and the Idaho Treatment Group.

“The goal of the event was to assist businesses, primarily small businesses, in understanding how to do work with the government and its contractors,” said Jennifer Cate, Small Business Program Manager for DOE-Idaho.

“Just as making connections between companies isn’t easy, it’s not always easy for businesses to understand how to connect with INL, whose role as a federal contractor is to provide maximum opportunities for small business,” said Stacey Francis, INL Small Business Program Manager.

“The benefit of the forum is that people learn how to get registered to do business with us and we can provide them tips on how to market to us,” Francis said. “Understanding what it takes to get that first proposal, or request for quote, however, is often a mystery,” she said.

“Events such as this Small Business Forum allow us to explain that process to interested vendors and take the mystery out of it,” Francis said. “It also allows us to set the expectation for the vendors on what we expect from our small business partners.”

As the third largest employer in the state, INL has a significant impact on the economy through its payroll and charitable contributions. Another large impact is made through the acquisition of goods and services within the state. The laboratory works with small businesses that support its research efforts. Managers look for businesses to provide lab equipment, office supplies, construction and other goods and services through subcontracts.

At the forum, guest speakers included Irene Gonzales, Economic Development Specialist with the U.S. Small Business Administration, and Gary Moore, Program Manager for the Idaho Procurement Technical Assistance Center. Gonzales and Moore shared the resources available to small businesses, and discussed various certifications required to perform work with the government.

Each year, INL negotiates with DOE-Idaho to establish small business goals for the coming year. For fiscal year 2015, the goal is to spend 40 percent of the procurement volume with small businesses. Within this goal are additional goals for socioeconomic classifications, such as Women-Owned, Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned, Small Disadvantaged Business and Historically Underutilized Business Zone. INL also has a goal to award 30 percent of its procurement dollars to Idaho businesses, and the lab has exceeded its 30 percent goal by spending 39 percent of the lab’s purchases in Idaho since 2005.

“Achieving the INL small business goal is a collective effort,” said Francis. “Our mission organizations are valuable partners to our small business program. Without their support for small business, achieving INL goals would be a significant challenge. We have many examples of where partnering with small business has been a win-win for all involved.”
In the past decade, INL purchased over $1 billon of lab equipment, office supplies, construction and other goods and services through subcontracts with Idaho businesses. Since Battelle Energy Alliance began managing the lab in 2005, it has engaged in partnerships with hundreds of businesses throughout Idaho.

One of INL's objectives is to target, shape and retain Idaho businesses as valued partners in supporting research happening at the lab.

"We are proud to work with so many innovative Idaho businesses," said Juan Alvarez, INL’s Deputy Laboratory Director for Management. "We are very fortunate to work with high-caliber businesses that can support our needs right here in Idaho."

DOE-Idaho Operations Office Manager Rick Provencher said, “The small businesses that support the nuclear energy program and the prime contractors at the Idaho site are integral to providing high-quality materials and services to the American taxpayer.” The amount of business volume has increased in recent years, and this has resulted in benefits to businesses, jobs and the Idaho economy. “I am pleased to see a high level of interest from the business community in doing work with DOE, and foresee a mutual benefit to them bidding on new work.”
